Fanny Dashwood

The Grasping Wife

Antagonist

Born Fanny Ferrars, now mistress of Norland Park, Fanny Dashwood combines cold selfishness with relentless determination. Her campaign to prevent her husband from helping his half-sisters opens the novel; her opposition to her brother Edward's attachment to Elinor complicates it further.

Character Overview

Fanny Dashwood enters the novel as a force of cold, calculating selfishness. Where her husband John has weak good intentions, Fanny has none at all. She sees the Dashwood sisters not as family to be helped but as rivals to be defeated‚Äîcompetitors for money that should belong to her son Harry.

"Mrs. John Dashwood had never been a favourite with any of her husband's family; but she had had no opportunity, till the present, of shewing them with how little attention to the comfort of other people she could act when occasion required it." ‚Äî Sense and Sensibility, on Fanny's true character

Her arrival at Norland before the old Mr. Dashwood is even buried demonstrates her priorities: securing property matters more than propriety. She immediately makes Mrs. Dashwood and her daughters feel like unwelcome guests in what was their home, driving them to seek accommodation elsewhere.

Yet Fanny maintains perfect respectability. She commits no obvious offenses against good manners; she simply pursues her interests with relentless efficiency. Society tolerates her because she violates no rules‚Äîonly expectations of basic human kindness.

Key Character Traits

Cold

Fanny lacks warmth entirely. Family feeling, gratitude, compassion‚Äîthese emotions seem absent from her makeup. She calculates; she does not feel.

Grasping

Every interaction reduces to financial calculation. What might be gained? What might be lost? Who threatens her son's inheritance?

Manipulative

She controls her weak husband through sustained argument, convincing him that selfishness is actually virtue, that meanness is prudence.

Snobbish

She judges others by wealth and status, despising her husband's half-sisters for their reduced circumstances and opposing connections beneath her.

The Art of Persuasion

The conversation in which Fanny talks John out of helping his sisters stands as one of Austen's most brilliant comic achievements. John begins resolved to give three thousand pounds‚Äîhis father's dying wish. Fanny proceeds to demolish this intention through a series of seemingly reasonable objections.

Three thousand is too much‚Äîthink of little Harry. An annuity then? But Mrs. Dashwood might live forever. Perhaps occasional gifts? But they have china and linen already. Really, any help would be excessive‚Äîthe sisters have five hundred pounds a year between them. They will live quite comfortably. John owes them nothing.

"Indeed, to say the truth, I am convinced within myself that your father had no idea of your giving them any money at all. The assistance he thought of, I dare say, was only such as might be reasonably expected of you." ‚Äî Fanny Dashwood, reinterpreting her father-in-law's dying wish

What makes the scene devastating is John's eager acceptance of each argument. He wants to be convinced; Fanny merely provides the logic. By conversation's end, he believes giving nothing constitutes generous attention to family duty.

Key Relationships

  • John Dashwood: Her weak-willed husband, whom she dominates completely. His good intentions cannot survive her determined opposition.
  • Edward Ferrars: Her brother, whose attachment to Elinor she despises and whose secret engagement to Lucy Steele sends her into hysterics.
  • Mrs. Ferrars: Her formidable mother, whose mercenary values Fanny shares and whose approval she cultivates.
  • The Dashwood Sisters: Her husband's half-sisters, whom she treats as rivals and drives from their home. She opposes any assistance to them.
  • Lucy Steele: Her initial favorite, cultivated for her flattery, then despised when her secret engagement to Edward emerges.

Thematic Significance

Fanny Dashwood embodies the mercenary values the novel critiques. For her, every relationship is transactional, every connection evaluated by financial return. Love, duty, gratitude‚Äîthese words have no meaning except as they serve material interests.

She represents the worst aspects of the property system that governs Austen's world. Inheritance law favors men; women's security depends on men's generosity. When that generosity fails‚Äîwhen men like John Dashwood allow wives like Fanny to override moral obligation‚Äîwomen suffer. The Dashwood sisters' precarious position results directly from Fanny's influence over her husband.

Yet Fanny faces no consequences for her behavior. She prospers, inherits further wealth, and maintains her position in society. Austen offers no poetic justice, no comeuppance for her villainy. This is perhaps the novel's bleakest observation: in a world that values property over principle, those who pursue property most ruthlessly often succeed.

Fanny also illuminates the limitations of "sense" divorced from feeling. She is perfectly rational‚Äîshe calculates interests, weighs advantages, pursues logical ends. But her rationality serves selfishness rather than virtue. True sense, the novel suggests, must be tempered by sensibility‚Äîby feeling for others, by recognition of moral obligation beyond material interest.
