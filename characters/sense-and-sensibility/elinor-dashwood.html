Elinor Dashwood

The Voice of Reason and Hidden Feeling

Protagonist

Elinor Dashwood

The eldest Dashwood sister at nineteen, Elinor represents "sense" in the novel's central dichotomy. Her rational nature and self-control allow her to support her family through hardship, even while concealing her own heartbreak over Edward Ferrars's secret engagement.

Character Overview

Elinor Dashwood is introduced as possessing "a strength of understanding, and coolness of judgment" that make her the family's practical anchor. At nineteen, she has already become the person to whom her emotional mother turns for advice and support.

Unlike her sister Marianne, Elinor believes in governing her emotions rather than being governed by them. She feels deeply‚Äîas deeply as Marianne‚Äîbut considers it inappropriate and ultimately harmful to display strong feelings openly. This self-discipline allows her to function in society while suffering privately.

Her relationship with Edward Ferrars tests her principles to the utmost. When she learns of his secret engagement to Lucy Steele, she must maintain her composure while her hopes are shattered, continuing to support her family without any outlet for her grief.

Sense vs. Sensibility

Elinor embodies "sense"‚Äîthe rational, controlled approach to life that the novel ultimately endorses without dismissing sensibility entirely. Her restraint is not coldness but strength; she feels as intensely as Marianne but chooses not to impose her feelings on others.

The contrast between the sisters is most evident in their responses to romantic disappointment. When Willoughby abandons Marianne, she surrenders to grief so completely that she nearly dies. When Elinor learns of Edward's engagement, she controls her anguish enough to continue caring for her family.

Yet Austen does not entirely favor sense over sensibility. Elinor's self-control sometimes becomes excessive, and her final breakdown when Edward is freed to marry her reveals how much emotion she has suppressed. The novel suggests balance rather than the triumph of one temperament over another.

Key Relationships

Edward Ferrars: The quiet, honorable man she loves. Their courtship is subdued, marked more by silent understanding than passionate declaration, but their mutual regard is deep and genuine.

Marianne Dashwood: Her younger sister, whose opposite temperament creates both conflict and complementarity. Elinor's steady presence helps Marianne through her crisis, while Marianne's intensity illuminates what Elinor conceals.

Lucy Steele: Her rival and tormentor, who reveals the secret engagement partly from jealousy and partly from malice. Elinor must maintain courtesy toward Lucy while bleeding internally.
