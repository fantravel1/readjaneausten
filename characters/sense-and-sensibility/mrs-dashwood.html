Mrs. Dashwood

The Romantic Mother

Family

The widowed mother of Elinor, Marianne, and Margaret, Mrs. Dashwood shares her middle daughter's romantic sensibility. Her emotional nature, while loving, sometimes prevents her from providing the practical guidance her daughters need during their trials.

Character Overview

Mrs. Dashwood enters the novel in grief, having lost both her husband and her home. The late Mr. Dashwood's estate passes to his son John from a previous marriage, leaving his widow and three daughters dependent on John's generosity‚Äîwhich proves minimal thanks to John's wife Fanny.

Her response to crisis reveals both her virtues and limitations. She feels insults keenly and wishes to leave Norland immediately; practical considerations barely register against emotional imperatives. When Sir John Middleton offers Barton Cottage, she accepts with enthusiasm, seeing romance in their reduced circumstances rather than hardship.

This temperament‚Äîwarm, impulsive, romantic‚Äîmakes Mrs. Dashwood a loving mother but an imperfect guide. She encourages Marianne's attachment to Willoughby without requiring clarity about his intentions. She trusts feeling over form, spontaneity over prudence. The consequences fall on her daughters.

Key Character Traits

Romantic

Mrs. Dashwood sees life through a romantic lens. She values passion over prudence, feeling over calculation, and encourages the same in her daughters.

Loving

Her devotion to her daughters is absolute. She feels their joys and sorrows as her own, perhaps too intensely to offer objective counsel.

Impractical

Practical matters‚Äîmoney, security, social convention‚Äîweigh lightly against feeling. Her impulses are generous but not always wise.

Proud

She feels slights deeply and refuses to importune those who have wronged her, even when practical necessity might suggest otherwise.

Mother of Sensibility

Mrs. Dashwood's relationship with her daughters illuminates the novel's central theme. Marianne, who shares her mother's sensibility, receives unconditional approval. Elinor, whose rational temperament provides the family's only practical anchor, receives love but not always understanding.

When Marianne falls for Willoughby, Mrs. Dashwood sees only romance‚Äîthe picturesque meeting, the shared tastes, the obvious mutual attraction. That no engagement is announced, that propriety is being stretched, that danger looms‚Äîthese concerns barely register. She trusts feeling, and Marianne's feelings are manifestly engaged.

"I have not wanted syllables where actions have spoken so plainly." ‚Äî Mrs. Dashwood, on Willoughby's supposed intentions

Meanwhile, Elinor's quieter attachment to Edward receives less maternal attention. Mrs. Dashwood likes Edward well enough, but his reserved manner doesn't spark her romantic imagination. She fails to see her eldest daughter's suffering because Elinor doesn't display it dramatically.

The novel ultimately vindicates Elinor's approach without wholly condemning Mrs. Dashwood's. Sensibility has its place‚Äîin art, in feeling, in the warmth of family affection. But sense must govern conduct, especially in a world where women's choices carry such lasting consequences.

Key Relationships

  • Elinor Dashwood: Her eldest daughter, whose rational temperament provides the practical guidance Mrs. Dashwood cannot. Their differing approaches create gentle tension.
  • Marianne Dashwood: Her middle daughter and emotional twin. Mrs. Dashwood's sensibility mirrors Marianne's, making her an enthusiastic supporter but poor counselor.
  • Margaret Dashwood: Her youngest daughter, still a child, whose romantic tendencies suggest she may follow Marianne's path.
  • John Dashwood: Her stepson, whose failure to provide for his father's widow and half-sisters wounds her pride as much as her purse.
  • Sir John Middleton: Her distant cousin who offers Barton Cottage and becomes a generous neighbor, his warmth matching her own.

Thematic Significance

Mrs. Dashwood embodies sensibility in its maternal form. She loves deeply, feels intensely, and trusts the heart's wisdom over the head's calculations. These qualities make her a warm, devoted mother‚Äîand an unreliable guide through the practical challenges her daughters face.

Her role illuminates the novel's exploration of female vulnerability. Widowed, dependent, and proud, she has few options and fewer protections. Her move to Barton Cottage represents not just reduced circumstances but reduced autonomy. In such a position, the luxury of sensibility becomes dangerous; feeling must be tempered by calculation if her daughters are to secure their futures.

Yet Austen treats Mrs. Dashwood with affection rather than censure. Her failings are the failings of a loving heart, not a cold one. If she errs in encouraging Marianne's imprudent attachment, she errs from warmth, not malice. The novel suggests that sense must govern, but it never mocks the sensibility it moderates.
