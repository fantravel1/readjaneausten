Character Overview

Marianne Dashwood is "sensible and clever, but eager in everything: her sorrows, her joys, could have no moderation." She believes in expressing every emotion fully and considers restraint a kind of dishonesty. Her romantic idealism shapes her expectations of love, poetry, and life itself.

At sixteen, Marianne has formed rigid opinions about what constitutes true feeling. She dismisses Colonel Brandon as too old for love at thirty-five and declares that a second attachment is impossible‚Äîone loves truly only once. These absolutes are tested by her experiences with Willoughby.

Her intensity, while genuine, proves dangerous. When Willoughby abandons her, she surrenders so completely to grief that she nearly dies. The illness that follows her heartbreak forces a reevaluation of her principles and opens her to a different kind of love.

Marianne and Willoughby

Marianne's romance with Willoughby begins like something from the novels she loves‚Äîhe rescues her after she falls on a rain-swept hillside, carrying her home in his arms. He shares her tastes in poetry, music, and romantic scenery. He seems the perfect match for her sensibility.

Their courtship proceeds with an openness that disturbs Elinor. Marianne sees no reason to hide her feelings; concealment would be false to her nature. She believes their attachment is understood, if not formally declared, and behaves accordingly.

Willoughby's sudden departure and eventual cruel letter shatter her world. His betrayal is not merely romantic disappointment but an assault on her entire philosophy‚Äîhow could such apparently genuine feeling be false? The revelation transforms her understanding of appearance and reality.

Growth and Transformation

Marianne's near-fatal illness marks the turning point in her development. Facing death, she recognizes how her self-indulgent grief has affected those who love her, particularly Elinor. She vows to live differently, governing her feelings rather than being governed by them.

Her eventual marriage to Colonel Brandon represents this transformation. The man she once dismissed as too old for passion proves capable of steady, enduring love. She learns that romantic intensity is not the only form feeling can take.

Yet the novel does not entirely condemn Marianne's sensibility. Her passion and expressiveness are genuine virtues; she merely needs to temper them with prudence. The ideal, Austen suggests, combines Elinor's sense with Marianne's depth of feeling.
