Character Overview

Sir Walter Elliot is vanity incarnate. His two great passions are his baronetcy and his appearance‚Äîhe studies the Baronetage for family pride and his mirror for personal admiration. These obsessions render him incapable of valuing anything that does not flatter his consequence or his looks.

Anne, his middle daughter, suffers most from his shallowness. She lacks Elizabeth's beauty and Mary's married status; she does not reflect well on Sir Walter, and so he ignores her. Her intelligence, her integrity, her genuine worth‚Äînone of these register with a father who can only see surfaces.

His financial irresponsibility forces the plot into motion. Years of extravagance beyond his income have created debts that require renting Kellynch Hall and retreating to Bath. Even this humiliation he manages to interpret as sophistication‚ÄîBath society is more elegant than country society, after all.

"Vanity was the beginning and the end of Sir Walter Elliot's character; vanity of person and of situation."
‚Äî The novel's opening description of Sir Walter

Key Character Traits

Vanity

Sir Walter's defining characteristic. He admires his own reflection, values only those who reflect well on him, and measures all worth by appearance and rank.

Snobbery

He despises anyone of inferior rank. His contempt for the navy‚Äîmen who have risen by merit rather than birth‚Äîreveals his shallow values.

Foolishness

Sir Walter lacks even basic intelligence. He is easily manipulated by flattery, blind to obvious deceptions, and incapable of learning from experience.

Neglect

His self-absorption leaves no room for genuine concern for others. He neglects Anne completely while spoiling Elizabeth, who flatters his vanity.

Extravagance

He spends far beyond his means to maintain appearances. His debts force the rental of Kellynch Hall‚Äîthe ultimate humiliation for one who values consequence.

Superficiality

Nothing matters to him but surfaces. He judges people by their complexions, their dress, their titles‚Äînever their characters or accomplishments.

The Baronetage

Sir Walter's favorite reading matter is the Baronetage‚Äîspecifically, the entry for "Elliot of Kellynch Hall." This book, which documents the family's noble lineage, provides him with endless self-satisfaction. He has even annotated the entry with the names of heirs and the dates of marriages.

This detail perfectly captures Austen's satire. Sir Walter reads only about himself; he finds no book so absorbing as the chronicle of his own consequence. His world extends no further than his own family tree, his own estate, his own reflection.

The Baronetage also represents the empty values Sir Walter embodies. It records only rank and connection, birth and death‚Äînothing of character, achievement, or worth. It is the perfect book for a man who can see nothing beyond these superficial distinctions.

"Sir Walter Elliot, of Kellynch Hall, in Somersetshire, was a man who, for his own amusement, never took up any book but the Baronetage."
‚Äî Opening line of Persuasion

Key Relationships

  • Anne Elliot: His neglected middle daughter, whose worth he cannot perceive. Anne's lack of beauty and low spirits make her invisible to a father who values only appearances.
  • Elizabeth Elliot: His favorite daughter, who shares his vanity and flatters his consequence. Their relationship is mutually shallow but mutually satisfying.
  • William Elliot: His heir, whose renewed attentions gratify his vanity. Sir Walter is easily deceived by William Elliot's smooth flattery.
  • Mrs. Clay: Elizabeth's companion, whose designs on Sir Walter everyone sees but himself. His susceptibility to flattery makes him easy prey.
  • Admiral Croft: His tenant at Kellynch Hall, whom Sir Walter despises for his weathered complexion and lack of rank, yet must tolerate for financial reasons.

Comedy and Critique

Sir Walter Elliot is funny‚Äîperhaps the funniest character Austen ever created. His objection to the navy because it destroys men's complexions, his horror at Mr. Shepherd's freckles, his counting of ugly women in Bath‚Äîthese moments of pure absurdity make him a comic masterpiece.

Yet beneath the comedy lies serious critique. Sir Walter represents the worst of the aristocratic class: its empty pride, its contempt for merit, its worship of birth over worth. In a novel that celebrates the self-made naval officers, Sir Walter embodies everything the navy's meritocracy opposes.

His treatment of Anne particularly indicts his values. Here is a daughter of genuine intelligence and feeling, and her father cannot see her at all. His blindness to Anne's worth measures the extent of his moral bankruptcy. He is not cruel so much as simply empty‚Äîthere is nothing inside him but vanity.

Sir Walter remains unchanged at the novel's end. He does not learn, does not grow, does not even recognize that anything has happened. Anne's happiness with Wentworth means nothing to him except that her husband is now wealthy enough to be acknowledged. His shallowness is impervious to experience, immune to wisdom. He will die as he lived: reading his own name in the Baronetage.
