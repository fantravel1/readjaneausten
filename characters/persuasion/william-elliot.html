Character Overview

William Elliot presents a puzzle that Anne must solve. His attentions to her are flattering, his manners impeccable, his apparent reformation from a dissolute past convincing. Lady Russell is charmed; Sir Walter is gratified; even Anne finds herself drawn to a man who seems to value her as her family does not.

Yet something about him troubles Anne from the start. His agreeableness is too complete, his opinions too accommodating. He says what everyone wants to hear, but Anne wonders what he actually thinks. His surface reveals nothing because there may be nothing beneath it‚Äîor something he wishes to hide.

Mrs. Smith's revelations confirm Anne's instincts. William Elliot is not reformed but merely calculating. His interest in the Elliot family is purely mercenary‚Äîhe wants to ensure no new heir displaces him. His attentions to Anne aim at securing her help in managing Sir Walter. His past treatment of Mrs. Smith and her husband reveals a man capable of genuine cruelty.

Key Character Traits

Surface Charm

William Elliot's manners are perfect‚Äîtoo perfect. His agreeableness to everyone should warn that he agrees with no one; he merely performs agreement.

Calculation

Every action serves his interests. His return to Sir Walter's favor, his attentions to Anne, his presence in Bath‚Äîall calculated to secure his inheritance.

Coldness

Beneath the warm manner lies genuine coldness. His treatment of the Smiths‚Äîexploiting their friendship, then abandoning them‚Äîreveals his true nature.

Mercenary Spirit

He married for money once and would do so again. His interest in Anne is not romantic but practical‚Äîshe would help him manage her father.

Adaptability

He can become whatever his audience requires. To Sir Walter, he values rank; to Anne, he values sense. Neither version is genuine.

Self-Interest

The only consistent thing about William Elliot is his dedication to his own advantage. Everything else‚Äîmanners, opinions, relationships‚Äîserves this single aim.

Character Arc

William Elliot's story begins before the novel opens, with his youthful estrangement from Sir Walter. He refused to court Elizabeth when encouraged, married instead for money, and expressed contempt for the baronetcy he stood to inherit. His former letter to Mr. Smith, scorning Sir Walter as a "fool," reveals his earlier attitude.

His apparent reformation raises hopes. He appears in Bath newly attentive to Sir Walter, flattering about the family name, interested in Anne. His explanation‚Äîthat maturity has taught him to value what he once scorned‚Äîseems plausible. Even Anne, who suspects something, cannot identify what troubles her.

Mrs. Smith provides the missing information. William Elliot's return to Sir Walter's favor is purely mercenary: he fears Sir Walter might remarry Mrs. Clay and produce a male heir who would displace him. His interest in Anne stems from her potential usefulness in preventing this. He has no genuine attachment to anyone or anything except his own advancement.

Exposed, William Elliot disappears from Bath with Mrs. Clay, presumably to use her as a tool in his ongoing schemes. His exit is appropriately anticlimactic‚Äîhe simply leaves when his plot fails, seeking other means to his ends. Unlike a Gothic villain, he faces no dramatic punishment, just the continuation of his cold, calculating existence.

Key Relationships

  • Anne Elliot: The object of his calculated pursuit. Anne's instinctive distrust of him proves justified when his mercenary motives are revealed.
  • Sir Walter Elliot: His foolish cousin, easily flattered into welcoming the heir who once scorned him. Sir Walter never suspects William Elliot's true motives.
  • Mrs. Smith: His former victim, whose husband he exploited and then abandoned. Her revelations to Anne expose his true character.
  • Mrs. Clay: His eventual accomplice, with whom he departs Bath. Their alliance suggests shared mercenary values and mutual usefulness.
  • Lady Russell: Anne's godmother, who is completely taken in by his manner. Her approval of him shows the limits of her judgment.

The Wickham of Persuasion

William Elliot invites comparison with Wickham from Pride and Prejudice: both are charming men whose surfaces conceal selfish calculation. But while Wickham is careless and impulsive, William Elliot is controlled and patient. His villainy is colder, more calculated, more dangerous.

Anne's ability to see through him where others fail reflects her mature judgment. Unlike Elizabeth Bennet, who initially trusted Wickham's charm, Anne is skeptical from the start. Her experience has taught her that perfect agreeableness often signals perfect emptiness.

William Elliot also serves a structural function: his pursuit of Anne creates the jealousy that finally pushes Wentworth to declare himself. Without the threat of losing Anne to her cousin, Wentworth might have maintained his proud distance indefinitely. William Elliot's villainy thus inadvertently enables the happy ending he sought to prevent.

His final fate‚Äîexile with Mrs. Clay, scheming presumably continuing‚Äîdenies readers the satisfaction of seeing him punished. But this refusal of dramatic justice is itself realistic. Men like William Elliot often prosper in the world, and Austen will not pretend otherwise. His continued existence on the novel's margins reminds us that not all stories end with the villain defeated.
