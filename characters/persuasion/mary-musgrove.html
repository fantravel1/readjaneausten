Mary Musgrove

The Perpetual Invalid

Family

Anne's youngest sister and the only Elliot daughter to marry, Mary is a comic creation whose constant complaints and imaginary illnesses provide much of Persuasion's humor. Yet beneath the comedy lies a portrait of a woman made miserable by her own self-absorption.

Character Overview

Mary Musgrove is Austen's portrait of petty unhappiness‚Äîa woman with every material blessing who remains perpetually dissatisfied. Her marriage to Charles Musgrove, heir to a respectable estate, should have brought contentment. Instead, Mary finds endless grievances: her health, her precedence, her in-laws, her children, her nerves.

"I am so ill I can hardly speak. I have not seen a creature the whole morning!" ‚Äî Mary Musgrove, Persuasion

The comedy of Mary lies in her complete lack of self-awareness. She genuinely believes herself to be "the most afflicted creature in the world" while Anne, who has genuine cause for unhappiness, suffers in silence. Mary's complaints are so constant and so obviously unfounded that they become absurd‚Äîa running joke that Austen maintains throughout the novel.

Yet Mary is not simply comic relief. She represents what happens when Elliot vanity meets limited intelligence. Unlike Elizabeth, who at least has beauty and position to support her self-regard, Mary has married beneath her family's expectations and feels the slight constantly. Her hypochondria is partly a strategy for gaining attention in a household that would otherwise overlook her.

Key Character Traits

Hypochondriac

Mary's imaginary illnesses are her primary means of commanding attention and sympathy. The moment Anne arrives, she recovers; the moment she's overlooked, she's ill again.

Self-Pitying

No one, in Mary's view, has ever suffered as she suffers. Her complaints are constant, varied, and completely divorced from her actual comfortable circumstances.

Status-Conscious

Mary inherited just enough Elliot pride to resent her position without the wit to make the best of it. She constantly worries about precedence and consequence.

Neglectful Mother

Her self-absorption extends to her children, who are spoiled, unruly, and largely left to servants and Anne. She wants the credit of motherhood without its duties.

Comic Function

Mary provides essential comic relief in a novel that deals with serious themes of lost love and second chances. Her petty complaints contrast sharply with Anne's genuine suffering, highlighting by comparison the depth of feeling that makes Anne admirable.

One of Austen's cleverest uses of Mary is in the Lyme episode. When young Charles injures himself seriously, Mary's response is characteristically self-centered‚Äîshe cannot possibly stay to nurse him because her nerves won't stand it, yet she also cannot possibly leave because she is his mother. The absurdity of her position, torn between competing selfishnesses, showcases Austen's satirical skill.

Mary's running competition with her in-laws‚Äîparticularly her resentment of Mrs. Musgrove's grandmotherly claims and the Musgrove sisters' social position‚Äîcreates a constant undercurrent of petty warfare. Her certainty that everyone is plotting against her consequence makes her a figure of fun, even as it isolates her from genuine connection.

Key Relationships

  • Anne Elliot: Her elder sister, whom she treats as a useful companion and nurse. Anne's visits "cure" Mary's ailments because they provide the attention she craves.
  • Charles Musgrove: Her husband, with whom she shares little genuine intimacy. Their marriage is comfortable but lacks depth on both sides.
  • The Musgrove Family: Her in-laws, whom she simultaneously looks down upon (for their inferior rank) and envies (for their warmth and connection).
  • Sir Walter & Elizabeth: Her father and sister, who largely ignore her. She resents their neglect while still craving their approval.
  • Her Children: Charles and Walter, whom she spoils and neglects by turns, neither disciplining them nor truly attending to them.

Thematic Significance

Mary Musgrove represents a different kind of failure than her father and elder sister. Where they fail through excessive pride in their position, Mary fails through inadequate appreciation of her blessings. She has what Elizabeth lacks‚Äîmarriage, children, a place in a warm family‚Äîand values none of it.

In the novel's economy of happiness, Mary's discontent serves as a counterpoint to Anne's patient endurance. Both sisters have reason to be unhappy: Anne has lost her love; Mary has found a merely adequate marriage. But where Anne grows through suffering into wisdom, Mary only compounds her unhappiness through constant complaint.

The novel ends with Mary satisfied‚Äînot because her circumstances have improved, but because Anne's marriage to Captain Wentworth gives her something to boast about. "Anne had no Uppercross Hall before her, no landed estate, no headship of a family," but Mary can claim connection to a rising naval hero. That this externality makes Mary happy says everything about her values‚Äîand nothing about genuine contentment.
