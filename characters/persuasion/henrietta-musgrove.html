Henrietta Musgrove

The Other Sister

Supporting

Louisa's elder sister, Henrietta Musgrove initially appears interchangeable with her sibling—equally lively, equally interested in Captain Wentworth. Yet her wavering between the glamorous Captain and her established attachment to Charles Hayter reveals a different character, one whose constancy ultimately proves stronger than her sister's.

Character Overview

Henrietta Musgrove and her sister Louisa form a seemingly inseparable pair when first introduced. Both are young, both are pretty, both are eager for entertainment. Captain Wentworth's arrival at Uppercross throws the household into excitement, and both sisters respond with obvious interest in the handsome naval officer.

"The Miss Musgroves were... very good-humoured, unaffected girls, but unformed in understanding... very much like thousands of other young ladies." — Persuasion, on Henrietta and Louisa

Yet Henrietta's situation differs from Louisa's in one crucial respect: she has an existing attachment. Charles Hayter, a respectable young curate and their cousin, has long been understood to have an understanding with Henrietta. Captain Wentworth's arrival threatens this nascent relationship, as Henrietta's attention wavers toward the more glamorous newcomer.

This wavering distresses Charles Hayter and provides Mary Musgrove with material for complaint. But Henrietta's inconstancy proves temporary. Unlike her sister, who commits fully to Wentworth only to be transformed by her fall, Henrietta returns to her original attachment and remains there.

Key Character Traits

Good-humored

Like her sister, Henrietta possesses an easy, pleasant temperament. She is agreeable company without being particularly distinguished.

Wavering

Her initial attraction to Wentworth despite her attachment to Hayter shows susceptibility to novelty and glamour.

Ultimately Constant

Unlike Louisa, Henrietta returns to her original attachment and remains faithful to Charles Hayter, achieving the constancy the novel values.

Conventional

She represents ordinary young womanhood—pleasant, amiable, unremarkable. Her story is the expected one of marrying as expected.

The Charles Hayter Connection

Charles Hayter occupies an awkward position in the Uppercross social world. Though a cousin to the Musgroves and a respectable clergyman, he lacks the dash and romantic appeal of a naval captain fresh from war. His courtship of Henrietta had seemed settled until Wentworth's arrival threw everything into doubt.

Henrietta's temporary neglect of Hayter creates one of the novel's minor romantic crises. Hayter feels slighted and withdraws; Henrietta seems oblivious to his pain. Only when Wentworth makes clear he has no intentions toward her—through the famous "nut" speech, often read as addressed to Louisa—does Henrietta recognize where her true affections lie.

"There is a quickness of perception in some, a nicety in the discernment of character, a natural penetration, in short, which no experience in others can equal." — Persuasion, on discerning character

Her return to Hayter suggests she never truly transferred her affections—she was merely dazzled. The glamour of Wentworth temporarily obscured her genuine feelings for the steadier, quieter man she had always preferred. In this, her trajectory mirrors the novel's larger argument: initial attraction matters less than lasting attachment.

Thematic Significance

Henrietta Musgrove's small romantic drama illuminates the novel's central concerns through a minor key. Her wavering between Hayter and Wentworth, her temporary dazzlement, and her ultimate constancy create a compressed version of the novel's argument about lasting attachment versus superficial attraction.

Significantly, Henrietta's story resolves happily through precisely the quality Wentworth criticizes in Anne: persuadability. Henrietta is persuaded away from Wentworth and back toward Hayter—not through direct intervention but through circumstances that reveal where her genuine affections lie. Her susceptibility to influence proves beneficial rather than damaging.

This outcome subtly challenges Wentworth's simplistic opposition between firmness and yielding. Henrietta's firmness would have led her astray; her yielding returns her to happiness. Sometimes being persuaded is wisdom rather than weakness. The contrast with Louisa, whose firm insistence on jumping leads to disaster, reinforces this point.

Henrietta ends the novel quietly engaged to Charles Hayter, her small drama resolved without crisis or transformation. In a story about second chances and recovered love, she represents the simpler path: recognizing one's true attachment before it's too late to act on it.
