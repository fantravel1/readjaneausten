Character Overview

Frederick Wentworth represents Austen's most romantic hero‚Äîa man whose feelings, unlike the reserved Darcy or the cool Knightley, run visibly deep. His bitterness at Anne's rejection, his determined pursuit of other women, his jealousy at William Elliot's attentions‚Äîall reveal a passionate nature barely under control.

Eight years before the novel begins, Wentworth was a young commander without fortune, his prospects uncertain despite his talents. Anne's rejection, though she yielded to persuasion rather than acting from her own feelings, wounded him deeply. He left determined to prove himself and to forget her.

He has succeeded in the first but failed in the second. The wars have made him wealthy; captured ships have provided the fortune he lacked. But Anne remains in his heart, and his return to her neighborhood forces him to confront feelings he thought he had conquered.

"You pierce my soul. I am half agony, half hope. Tell me not that I am too late, that such precious feelings are gone for ever."

Key Character Traits

Pride

Wentworth's wounded pride drives much of the novel's tension. He cannot easily forgive Anne's rejection, though he still loves her.

Passion

Unlike Austen's more restrained heroes, Wentworth feels openly and deeply. His letter to Anne is the most passionate declaration in her novels.

Honor

Despite his feelings, Wentworth behaves honorably throughout. He believes himself bound to Louisa and is prepared to marry her until she releases him.

Constancy

For all his attempts to forget Anne, he has never truly stopped loving her. His constancy matches her own.

Professional Excellence

He is a superb naval officer whose skill and daring have made his fortune. The navy represents merit over birth‚Äîhis values against the Elliots'.

Capacity for Growth

Wentworth must learn to forgive, to let go of his resentment, to see that Anne's submission to persuasion came from duty, not indifference.

Character Arc

Wentworth's return to Anne's world sets the novel in motion. He arrives at Kellynch as tenant to the Crofts, determined to marry‚Äîanyone but Anne. His pointed attentions to the Musgrove sisters, particularly the lively Louisa, seem designed to wound Anne while protecting himself.

Yet his behavior betrays feelings he cannot entirely suppress. Small kindnesses‚Äîremoving her troublesome nephew, ensuring she has a seat in the carriage‚Äîreveal continuing care. His anger at finding her in William Elliot's company suggests jealousy he has no right to feel.

The accident at Lyme Regis proves transformative. When Louisa falls and lies unconscious, it is Anne whose calm competence manages the crisis. Wentworth sees, perhaps for the first time, what he lost eight years ago‚Äînot just a pretty girl but a woman of genuine strength and judgment.

The months of Louisa's recovery give him time to recognize his mistake. He was attracted to Louisa's spirited resolution, her refusal to be persuaded‚Äîbut he comes to see that such firmness can become mere stubbornness, while Anne's yielding to persuasion reflected proper deference, not weakness of character.

Key Relationships

  • Anne Elliot: The love of his life, whom he could not forget despite eight years of determined effort. Their reunion represents the novel's emotional heart.
  • Mrs. Croft (Sophia): His devoted sister, whose happy marriage to Admiral Croft represents the partnership possible between equals. She provides him both family connection and example.
  • Louisa Musgrove: The young woman whose spirited determination initially attracts him, until her accident reveals the dangers of such firmness unchecked by judgment.
  • Captain Harville: His brother officer and close friend, in whose home the crucial conversation about constancy takes place. Harville's loyalty represents the bonds of naval service.
  • Captain Benwick: A fellow officer whose rapid transfer of affection from the dead Fanny Harville to the recovering Louisa comments ironically on the nature of constancy.

The Letter

Captain Wentworth's letter to Anne is the most famous passage in Austen's work, the moment when his reserve finally breaks and his true feelings pour out. Written in haste while Anne speaks to Harville about women's constancy, the letter declares what Wentworth has been unable to say aloud.

"You pierce my soul" announces a lover who can no longer hide behind pride or resentment. The phrase "half agony, half hope" captures perfectly the torment of loving without knowing if one is loved in return. His assertion that he has "loved none but you" and been "never inconstant" answers Anne's own claims about women's constancy with equal devotion.

The letter represents Wentworth's final transformation. The proud man who returned to wound Anne with his indifference becomes the humble suitor who can only "lay at your feet" and await her answer. His willingness to be vulnerable, to risk rejection again, proves his love more eloquently than any protestation.

This declaration makes Wentworth unique among Austen's heroes. Darcy's proposal, even his second one, remains somewhat formal; Knightley's confession happens largely off-page. Only Wentworth speaks with the full passionate intensity of romantic love, and his letter remains the emotional climax toward which the entire novel moves.
