The Question of Constancy

Benwick's engagement to Louisa raises the novel's central question from a new angle: what is constancy worth? Anne has remained faithful to her feelings for Wentworth for eight years; Benwick's devotion to Fanny Harville proves more fragile. Does this make Anne admirable and Benwick shallow, or does it simply show that people recover from grief at different rates?

Austen refuses to resolve this question definitively. Captain Wentworth is shocked and somewhat contemptuous of Benwick's rapid recovery‚Äî"A man does not recover from such a devotion of the heart to such a woman! He ought not; he does not." Yet Wentworth himself has formed new attachments during his separation from Anne, however unsuccessfully.

Anne's response is more measured. She recognizes that Benwick's temperament‚Äîsensitive, dependent on companionship‚Äîmakes him particularly vulnerable to new attachment. She does not condemn him, though she may quietly feel the superiority of her own constancy. The novel leaves readers to form their own judgment.

Thematic Significance

Captain Benwick serves as a crucial foil in a novel about constancy and change. His example allows Austen to explore the complexity of lasting feeling without reducing it to simple categories of faithful and faithless. Grief, the novel suggests, works differently in different temperaments.

Benwick also represents a certain kind of Romantic sensibility that Austen views with gentle skepticism. His love of melancholy poetry may express genuine feeling, but it may also substitute for genuine feeling‚Äîproviding scripts for emotion rather than emotion itself. Anne's advice to read "more prose" suggests the danger of feeding grief rather than learning to live with it.

Finally, Benwick's match with Louisa quietly reinforces the novel's values. Louisa, whose fall from the Cobb transformed her from lively to subdued, seems now suited to the quiet, literary Benwick in a way she never was to the energetic Wentworth. The match may lack the grand passion of Anne and Wentworth's reunion, but it promises its own kind of companionable happiness.
