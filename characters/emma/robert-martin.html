Robert Martin

The Worthy Farmer

Supporting

A respectable, sensible young farmer whose genuine love for Harriet Smith is nearly destroyed by Emma's snobbish interference, Robert Martin represents honest merit that transcends social pretension. Mr. Knightley's high opinion of him proves entirely justified.

Character Overview

Robert Martin appears early in Emma as the young farmer who has captured Harriet Smith's heart during her stay at Abbey-Mill Farm. He is introduced through Harriet's glowing accounts: sensible, well-informed, kind to his mother and sisters, and devoted to Harriet herself. Everything about him suggests solid worth.

"He is very plain, undoubtedly—remarkably plain:—but that is nothing, compared with his entire want of gentility." — Emma Woodhouse, dismissing Robert Martin

Emma's dismissal of Martin reveals her own limitations rather than his. She judges by superficial standards—manners, address, social position—while ignoring the substantive qualities that matter. Mr. Knightley's strong defense of Martin challenges Emma's prejudices directly, insisting that the young farmer is "a respectable, intelligent gentleman-farmer" who "always speaks to the purpose."

Martin's letter proposing to Harriet proves his worth more conclusively than any character reference. Emma expects to find it awkward and illiterate; instead, she must acknowledge it is "a good letter... much better than I expected." Her surprise reveals her assumptions; the letter's quality challenges them.

Key Character Traits

Sensible

Martin speaks and writes with intelligence and purpose. He reads agricultural reports and manages his farm well. His sense is practical, not pretentious.

Constant

Despite Harriet's rejection, he proposes again when given the opportunity. His affection for her survives Emma's interference and Harriet's foolish pretensions.

Unpretentious

He is what he appears to be—a respectable farmer without social ambitions beyond his station. This honesty contrasts with Emma's matchmaking fantasies.

Worthy

Mr. Knightley's consistent praise confirms what Harriet instinctively recognized: Martin possesses genuine merit that social snobbery cannot diminish.

The Rejected Proposal

Robert Martin's proposal to Harriet Smith becomes the novel's first test of Emma's judgment—a test she fails spectacularly. Without ever meeting Martin, she has convinced herself that Harriet deserves better, that a farmer's wife is beneath what Harriet's beauty and sweetness merit. She persuades Harriet to refuse him.

Mr. Knightley's response is swift and sharp. He tells Emma directly that she has done Harriet "a very unkind office" and that Martin is exactly the kind of husband Harriet needs. The argument that follows is their first major conflict, and Knightley is entirely right.

"A young farmer... is the very last sort of person to raise my curiosity. The yeomanry are precisely the order of people with whom I feel I can have nothing to do." — Emma Woodhouse, revealing her snobbery

The rejected proposal haunts the novel. Harriet's subsequent humiliations—her false hopes about Mr. Elton, her inappropriate attraction to Mr. Knightley—all stem from Emma's interference. Every time Harriet suffers romantic disappointment, the reader remembers the sensible farmer she was persuaded to refuse.

Thematic Significance

Robert Martin represents the novel's argument that genuine worth matters more than social position. He lacks the superficial graces Emma values—elegance, fashion, romantic mystery—but possesses the substantial qualities that make for lasting happiness: sense, kindness, constancy, and honest industry.

His eventual marriage to Harriet provides the novel's most unambiguous happy ending. Unlike the complicated matches that precede it, this union is simply right: two good-hearted, unpretentious people who genuinely love each other, united against all odds and Emma's worst interference. That Harriet ends up exactly where she would have been had Emma never meddled reinforces the futility of social pretension.

Martin also represents Mr. Knightley's values against Emma's. Where Emma judges by surface, Knightley judges by substance. Where Emma imagines Harriet elevated to improbable heights, Knightley sees clearly that Abbey-Mill Farm offers the best life available to her. The novel's resolution vindicates Knightley's judgment entirely.

In the end, Robert Martin achieves everything through merit that Emma's fanciful scheming could never accomplish. He wins Harriet not through romantic gestures or social maneuvering but through steady worth that survives every obstacle. His success is the novel's quiet rebuke to Emma's snobbery.
