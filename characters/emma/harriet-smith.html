Harriet Smith

The Innocent Victim of Matchmaking

Character Overview

Harriet Smith is a sweet, pretty, simple girl of seventeen, the "natural daughter of somebody"‚Äîan illegitimate child whose parentage is unknown. She lives at Mrs. Goddard's school and becomes Emma's chosen project and companion.

Her defining quality is impressionability. She has no firm opinions of her own and readily adopts whatever views Emma suggests. This makes her perfect for Emma's matchmaking schemes but ensures she will be hurt by them.

Harriet's heart is genuine but easily redirected. She loves Robert Martin until Emma convinces her she can do better; then she believes herself in love with Mr. Elton, then Frank Churchill, then‚Äîmost alarmingly for Emma‚ÄîMr. Knightley. Only at the end does she return to her first love.

A Pawn in Emma's Games

Harriet's role in the novel is largely to expose Emma's flaws. Emma's encouragement of Harriet to refuse Robert Martin‚Äîa good man who genuinely loves her‚Äîreveals Emma's snobbery and interference. Her blindness to Mr. Elton's true object shows her self-deception.

Each of Harriet's romantic disappointments is a direct result of Emma's meddling. Had Emma never befriended her, Harriet would have married Robert Martin in the first volume and been perfectly happy. Emma's "improvement" of Harriet only makes her miserable.

Yet Harriet is not merely passive. Her eventual confession of love for Knightley forces Emma's crucial self-recognition. In trying to raise Harriet above her station, Emma has created a rival for the man she loves‚Äîa fitting irony.

The Happy Ending

Harriet's marriage to Robert Martin at the novel's end represents the restoration of natural order. She returns to the man who first loved her, whose social station matches her own, and who can make her genuinely happy.

The discovery that her father is merely a tradesman‚Äînot the gentleman Emma imagined‚Äîunderscores the folly of Emma's schemes. Harriet was never destined for high society; Emma's attempts to elevate her only postponed the happiness that was always within reach.
