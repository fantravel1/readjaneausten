Lydia Bennet

The Wild Child of Longbourn

Family

Lydia Bennet

The youngest Bennet daughter at fifteen, Lydia is wild, thoughtless, and boy-crazy. Her reckless elopement with Wickham creates the novel's central crisis, threatening to ruin the entire family. She represents the dangers of poor parenting and unchecked youthful impulses.

Character Overview

Lydia Bennet is a portrait of adolescent folly given free rein. At fifteen, she is "stout, well-grown," with a bold manner and high animal spirits. Her entire focus is on officers, bonnets, and fun‚Äîshe has no thoughts beyond immediate gratification and no understanding of consequences.

Her character is largely the product of her upbringing. Her mother encourages her worst tendencies, while her father cannot be bothered to discipline her. She has been "out" in society since she was fourteen‚Äîunusually early‚Äîand considers herself superior to her older sisters because of her height and boldness.

Lydia is not malicious; she is simply thoughtless. She does not mean to harm her family through her elopement‚Äîit never occurs to her to think about the effect on others at all. Her self-centeredness is complete but innocent of deliberate cruelty.

The Elopement

Lydia's elopement with Wickham is the novel's crisis point. She runs away with him from Brighton, convinced they will marry, apparently unaware that Wickham has no intention of doing so. Without Darcy's intervention, she would have been ruined‚Äîand her family with her.

The elopement reveals everything about Lydia's character. Her letter to Mrs. Forster shows no awareness of wrongdoing: "You need not send them word at Longbourn of my going, if you do not like it, for it will make the surprise the greater, when I write to them and sign my name 'Lydia Wickham.'" She sees the whole affair as a great joke.

Even after marriage, Lydia shows no shame or gratitude. She returns to Longbourn "as noisy as ever, unabashed, wild, noisy, and fearless," apparently unaware of how close she came to permanent disgrace. Her obliviousness to the havoc she has caused is both comic and disturbing.

Thematic Significance

Lydia represents the consequences of parental failure. Her wildness is not inherent but cultivated‚Äîor rather, allowed to grow unchecked‚Äîby parents who should have known better. She is what happens when youth, beauty, and high spirits meet indulgent mothering and indifferent fathering.

She also serves as a contrast to Elizabeth. Both sisters are bold and spirited, but Elizabeth's intelligence and moral grounding direct her energy productively, while Lydia's identical energy, without guidance, leads to disaster. Nature needs nurture to flourish properly.

Lydia's fate‚Äîmarried to Wickham, living on insufficient income, bored with her husband within months‚Äîis a quiet punishment. She got what she wanted and found it wanting. The novel's epilogue suggests an unhappy future of debt, restlessness, and decline.
