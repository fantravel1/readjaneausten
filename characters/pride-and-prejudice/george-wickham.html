George Wickham

The Charming Deceiver

Antagonist

George Wickham

The handsome, charming militia officer whose smooth manners and sympathetic story deceive Elizabeth and most of Meryton society. Wickham serves as a foil to Darcy, demonstrating that attractive exteriors can mask moral corruption, while reserve may hide genuine worth.

Character Overview

George Wickham is Austen's most fully realized villain, dangerous precisely because he is so appealing. His handsome face, easy manners, and ability to say exactly what others want to hear make him irresistible to the inhabitants of Meryton‚Äîand initially to Elizabeth Bennet herself.

Wickham grew up on the Pemberley estate as the son of the elder Mr. Darcy's steward. He was a favorite of Darcy's father, who provided for his education and intended to give him a valuable church living. This background makes his tale of being wronged by the younger Darcy seem credible to those who hear it.

What makes Wickham truly dangerous is not merely his dishonesty but his understanding of human nature. He knows how to present himself as a victim, how to inspire sympathy, and how to make others believe what he wants them to believe. His villainy lies not in violence but in manipulation and betrayal of trust.

True History with the Darcys

The truth about Wickham, revealed in Darcy's letter, paints a picture of a man whose pleasant exterior has always concealed a vicious character. Though the elder Mr. Darcy supported him generously, Wickham squandered every opportunity.

He refused the church living intended for him, taking ¬£3,000 instead, which he quickly gambled and spent away. When the money was gone, he demanded the living after all, and when refused, he began spreading lies about Darcy's treatment of him.

Most damningly, Wickham attempted to elope with Georgiana Darcy when she was just fifteen, hoping to gain her fortune of ¬£30,000. Only Darcy's timely intervention prevented the scheme. This attempted seduction of a girl he had known since childhood reveals the depth of his moral bankruptcy.

Role in the Plot

Wickham serves multiple functions in the novel. His lies about Darcy reinforce Elizabeth's initial prejudice, preventing her from seeing Darcy clearly. Her willingness to believe Wickham‚Äîa stranger‚Äîover Darcy illustrates how first impressions can mislead.

His elopement with Lydia creates the novel's crisis, threatening the Bennet family with social ruin. That Darcy secretly resolves this situation‚Äîpaying Wickham's debts and essentially bribing him to marry Lydia‚Äîdemonstrates Darcy's true character more powerfully than any speech could.

Wickham ultimately receives a kind of justice: he is tied forever to the foolish Lydia, living on insufficient income, his debts mounting and his charm fading. His punishment is to become exactly what he is, with no hope of escaping the consequences of his character.

Wickham's Legacy

Wickham established the template for a particular kind of Austen villain: the charming seducer whose attractive surface conceals moral rot. Willoughby in Sense and Sensibility and Henry Crawford in Mansfield Park follow similar patterns, though with their own variations.

He also serves as a warning about the dangers of trusting first impressions‚Äîthe very error Elizabeth must learn to overcome. Her initial preference for Wickham over Darcy shows how easily charm can be mistaken for character, and how reserve can be misread as coldness.
