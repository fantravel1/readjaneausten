Kitty Bennet

The Impressionable Follower

Character Overview

Kitty Bennet exists in Lydia's shadow. Though older than her youngest sister, she follows Lydia's lead in everything‚Äîthe pursuit of officers, the giggling over bonnets, the trips to Meryton. Where Lydia is the bold initiator, Kitty is the willing accomplice.

"Kitty, who took all these threats in a serious light, began to cry." ‚Äî Pride and Prejudice, on Kitty's response to Mr. Bennet's restrictions

Her tears when Mr. Bennet threatens to restrict her activities reveal both her dependence on Lydia and her more malleable nature. Lydia would rage; Kitty cries. This very weakness‚Äîher inability to stand alone‚Äîeventually becomes her salvation, as she proves equally susceptible to good influences once Lydia's are removed.

Throughout the novel, Kitty functions largely as Lydia's echo. She wants what Lydia wants, goes where Lydia goes, and cares about what Lydia cares about. Her personality is almost entirely derivative, defined by her association with her more forceful sister.

Key Character Traits

Impressionable

Kitty adopts the values and behaviors of whoever she's with. Under Lydia's influence, she's silly; under Jane and Elizabeth's, she improves.

Subordinate

She always plays second fiddle, never initiating mischief but always participating. She lacks the force of personality to lead.

Emotional

Quick to tears when threatened or disappointed, Kitty's emotions sit close to the surface, making her vulnerable but also reachable.

Redeemable

Unlike Lydia, whose self-absorption seems fixed, Kitty's character can be shaped by proper influence. She has no strong vices of her own.

Lydia's Shadow

The relationship between Kitty and Lydia illustrates how strong personalities can dominate weaker ones. Lydia, though younger, has always been the leader‚Äîmore daring, more determined, more selfish. Kitty follows because she lacks the resources to do otherwise.

When Lydia goes to Brighton without her, Kitty is devastated. Her grief is genuine if trivial‚Äîshe has lost her purpose, her guide, her reason for being. Without Lydia, she doesn't know who she is or what to do with herself.

"I cannot see why Mrs. Forster should not ask me as well as Lydia... I have a much better right to be asked than she has." ‚Äî Kitty Bennet, jealous of Lydia's Brighton invitation

Yet this very dependence contains the seeds of Kitty's redemption. She needs someone to follow; once separated from Lydia, she can follow better examples. Her personality is not corrupt, merely unformed. Given proper models, she can develop properly.

Thematic Significance

Kitty Bennet demonstrates the power of influence‚Äîfor good or ill. She is what her companions make her. Under Lydia's tutelage, she becomes a silly, officer-obsessed girl whose behavior embarrasses her sensible sisters. Under Jane and Elizabeth's guidance, she improves considerably.

The novel's final pages describe Kitty's reformation: "Kitty, to her very material advantage, spent the chief of her time with her two elder sisters. In society so superior to what she had generally known, her improvement was great." This brief summary confirms what her character has suggested throughout‚Äîshe is salvageable because she has no fixed vices, only borrowed ones.

Kitty also illustrates the Bennet parenting failure from a different angle. Where Lydia was spoiled into willfulness, Kitty was neglected into dependence. Neither girl received the guidance that might have formed better characters. That Kitty improves once properly supervised suggests what might have been possible had her parents taken more care.

In the economy of the novel, Kitty provides a middle term between Lydia's irredeemable silliness and the older sisters' mature sense. She demonstrates that character is not entirely fixed‚Äîthat proper influences can reshape even a badly formed mind, so long as reform comes before habits become immutable.
