Charlotte Lucas

Character Overview

Charlotte Lucas is introduced as Elizabeth's closest friend outside her family‚Äî"a sensible, intelligent young woman, about twenty-seven." Her age is significant: at twenty-seven, Charlotte is dangerously close to permanent spinsterhood by Regency standards, with no fortune to compensate for her plain appearance.

From the beginning, Charlotte's views on marriage differ from Elizabeth's. She advises Jane to show more affection to Bingley than she feels, arguing that a woman must make opportunities for herself. Her philosophy is practical rather than romantic: "Happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance."

This pragmatism sets up her surprising decision to accept Mr. Collins. Where Elizabeth sees only his absurdity, Charlotte sees security. Her choice forces Elizabeth‚Äîand the reader‚Äîto confront the harsh economic realities facing women of limited means in Regency England.

"I am not romantic, you know; I never was. I ask only a comfortable home; and considering Mr. Collins's character, connection, and situation in life, I am convinced that my chance of happiness with him is as fair as most people can boast on entering the marriage state."

The Marriage Decision

Charlotte's acceptance of Mr. Collins shocks Elizabeth, who cannot understand how her intelligent friend could marry such a ridiculous man. Elizabeth's reaction reveals her own romantic assumptions: she cannot imagine marriage without love and respect.

But Charlotte's reasoning is sound within her circumstances. At twenty-seven, with no beauty, fortune, or prospects, she faces a future of dependency on her family. Mr. Collins offers a comfortable home, respectable status, and‚Äîcrucially‚Äîan establishment of her own. These are not nothing.

When Elizabeth visits Hunsford, she sees how Charlotte manages her marriage. Charlotte has arranged the house so that she need not spend much time with her husband; she encourages his gardening and his time at Rosings. It is not a happy marriage, but it is a functional one, and Charlotte is "tolerably composed."

Thematic Significance

Charlotte's choice illuminates the novel's central themes of marriage and economic necessity. Her acceptance of Mr. Collins shows what Elizabeth refuses: a marriage of convenience without love. This makes Elizabeth's eventual acceptance of Darcy more meaningful‚Äîshe holds out for love and gets it.

Yet Austen does not condemn Charlotte. The narration treats her with sympathy, acknowledging that her choice, while not ideal, is reasonable given her options. Charlotte is not a romantic heroine, but she is not a fool either. She has made the best of a limited situation.

Charlotte's marriage also highlights the difference between Elizabeth's situation and that of many women. Elizabeth can afford her romantic ideals because she has options‚Äîyouth, wit, beauty. Charlotte, lacking these advantages, must be practical or risk lifelong dependency.
