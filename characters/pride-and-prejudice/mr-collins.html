Character Overview

Mr. Collins is a masterpiece of comic characterization. From his first letter to the Bennets‚Äîa marvel of pompous self-congratulation and condescension‚Äîhe establishes himself as a figure of pure absurdity. Every word he speaks reveals his vanity, his sycophancy, and his utter lack of self-awareness.

Austen describes him as "not a sensible man, and the deficiency of nature had been but little assisted by education or society." His father kept him "in subjection," and this repression has emerged in adulthood as an aggressive need to assert his importance and respectability.

His defining characteristic is his slavish devotion to his patroness, Lady Catherine de Bourgh. He speaks of her with reverence, quotes her opinions as sacred truths, and genuinely believes that her condescension to notice him is a mark of his own worth rather than her self-importance.

The Proposal

Mr. Collins's proposal to Elizabeth is one of the most comic scenes in English literature. He approaches it as a business transaction, listing his reasons for marrying with no reference to affection: it is right for a clergyman to marry; Lady Catherine has recommended it; and he wishes to make amends for inheriting Longbourn.

His certainty of acceptance is monumental. When Elizabeth refuses him, he assumes she is being coy‚Äî"the usual practice of elegant females." It takes repeated, increasingly firm rejections before he begins to believe she means it, and even then he attributes her refusal to a desire to "increase his love by suspense."

His rapid transfer of affections to Charlotte Lucas‚Äîwithin three days of Elizabeth's rejection‚Äîreveals that his "feelings" were never engaged at all. Marriage for Mr. Collins is entirely about his own consequence, not about the woman involved.

Comic Function

Mr. Collins provides some of the novel's richest comedy, but he also serves serious thematic purposes. His proposal to Elizabeth clarifies what she refuses to accept: a marriage without love or respect, entered into for purely practical reasons.

His character also satirizes the church of Austen's day. Mr. Collins has no genuine religious feeling; his concern is entirely with the social aspects of his position. His sermons, we learn, are composed with an eye to flattering Lady Catherine rather than edifying his congregation.

His marriage to Charlotte shows what happens when a sensible woman accepts a foolish man for economic security. Charlotte's pragmatic acceptance of her situation‚Äîcreating spaces where she need not see her husband‚Äîis both comic and slightly melancholy.
