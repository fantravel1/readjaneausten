Caroline Bingley

Bingley's snobbish, ambitious sister who pursues Darcy relentlessly while looking down on the Bennet family. Her scheming to separate Bingley from Jane and her barely concealed hostility toward Elizabeth make her a memorable minor antagonist.

Character Overview

Caroline Bingley represents the worst aspects of social climbing. Her fortune came from trade‚Äîher father made the family money in business‚Äîyet she looks down on anyone she considers beneath her, including people with much older pedigrees than her own.

Her primary motivation is to marry Darcy, which would elevate her social status significantly. To this end, she flatters him constantly, agrees with everything he says, and does her best to eliminate any competition. Her pursuit is so transparent that it becomes comic, especially as Darcy remains utterly indifferent to her charms.

Caroline's snobbery is particularly ironic given her own origins. She despises the Bennets for their inferior connections and "low" relations in trade, apparently forgetting that her own fortune comes from exactly such connections. Her social pretensions are built on foundations she refuses to acknowledge.

Scheming Against Jane

Caroline's most significant action in the novel is her participation in separating Bingley from Jane. She convinces her brother that Jane does not return his feelings and keeps Jane's presence in London secret from him, actively working against a match she considers beneath her family.

Her treatment of Jane when Jane visits London reveals her true character. Despite professing friendship, Caroline makes Jane wait weeks for a return visit, then treats her with cold civility. Her false friendship is crueler than open hostility would be.

Caroline's scheming ultimately fails. Bingley marries Jane, Darcy marries Elizabeth, and Caroline is "obliged to put up with" Elizabeth as a sister-in-law. Her social ambitions are thwarted, and she must make the best of a situation she finds deeply galling.

Foil to Elizabeth

Caroline serves as a foil to Elizabeth, highlighting the heroine's superior qualities. Where Caroline flatters Darcy, Elizabeth challenges him. Where Caroline is artificial and calculating, Elizabeth is natural and sincere. Darcy's preference for Elizabeth over Caroline demonstrates that he values substance over show.

The contrast between them is particularly evident in Caroline's attempts to diminish Elizabeth in Darcy's eyes. Every criticism she makes‚Äîof Elizabeth's muddy petticoat, her family's connections, her "fine eyes"‚Äîbackfires. Darcy finds Elizabeth more attractive, not less, when Caroline draws attention to her.

Caroline's fate at the novel's end is appropriately inglorious. She remains unmarried, her pursuit of Darcy having come to nothing. She must maintain a friendship with Elizabeth to preserve her relationship with Darcy‚Äîa bitter pill for someone who so despised her.
