Mr. Rushworth

The Wealthy Fool

Supporting

The owner of Sotherton Court and Maria Bertram's unfortunate husband, Mr. Rushworth possesses twelve thousand pounds a year and very little sense. His wealth makes him a desirable match; his stupidity makes the marriage a disaster waiting to happen.

Novel
Mansfield Park (1814)
Estate
Sotherton Court
Income
£12,000 per year
Marries
Maria Bertram

Character Overview

Mr. Rushworth enters the novel as Maria Bertram's fiance, a match celebrated by everyone except those who value intelligence. His wealth and estate are impressive; his conversation is not. He can barely remember his lines for the theatrical performance, and his observations rarely rise above the obvious.

"If this man had not twelve thousand a year, he would be a very stupid fellow." — Mary Crawford's assessment of Mr. Rushworth

Mary Crawford's dismissal is harsh but accurate. Rushworth's money is his only recommendation. He is not cruel or vicious—merely empty, a man whose wealth has never required him to develop either mind or character. He drifts through life on inherited fortune, noticed only for what he owns.

His preoccupation with "improving" Sotherton—by which he means following the fashionable landscape designer Repton—captures his character perfectly. He cannot form original opinions about his own estate; he needs an expert to tell him what to value. This same passivity marks his courtship, his marriage, and his eventual cuckolding.

Key Character Traits

Stupid

Austen does not soften this assessment. Rushworth lacks wit, perception, and understanding. His two-and-forty speeches in Lovers' Vows remain beyond his capacity.

Wealthy

His twelve thousand a year and his estate at Sotherton are his only attractions. Without them, he would be merely ridiculous rather than eligible.

Vain

His concern about his costume for the play—particularly his pink satin cloak—reveals shallow vanity about appearance masking deeper emptiness.

Oblivious

He fails to notice Maria's contempt, Henry Crawford's flirtation, or the general low opinion others hold of him. His blindness is nearly absolute.

The Doomed Marriage

Maria Bertram's engagement to Mr. Rushworth is purely mercenary. She wants his money, his estate, and the consequence of being Mrs. Rushworth of Sotherton Court. She does not want him. Her contempt for her fiance becomes obvious during the visit to Sotherton, where she visibly prefers Henry Crawford's company to her future husband's.

The theatrical episode makes the mismatch even clearer. While Maria and Henry rehearse their romantic scenes with inappropriate enthusiasm, Rushworth struggles to remember lines and worries about his costume. He senses something wrong but lacks the intelligence to identify it. His jealousy is real but ineffective—he cannot compete with Crawford's wit and charm.

"I think it is very foolish of me, and I see how foolish it is. But if I do not think of it myself, I do not know how I am to do it." — Mr. Rushworth on learning his theatrical lines

The marriage proceeds despite every warning sign. Maria, having failed to attract Henry Crawford, accepts Rushworth as her means of escape from Mansfield. The wedding occurs off-page, without celebration or joy. From the beginning, it is a match waiting to fail.

Thematic Significance

Mr. Rushworth embodies the novel's critique of marriages based solely on money and position. He offers Maria everything society values—wealth, estate, consequence—and nothing she actually needs: companionship, understanding, or love. The disaster that follows demonstrates that economic calculation cannot substitute for genuine compatibility.

His stupidity also serves thematic purposes. In a novel concerned with moral education and discernment, Rushworth represents the complete absence of these qualities. He cannot judge character, cannot understand situations, cannot even learn theatrical lines. His mind is a blank that his money cannot fill.

The novel treats him as both comic and pathetic. His worries about his pink cloak are funny; his eventual humiliation is not. When Maria elopes with Henry Crawford, Rushworth becomes a victim of her contempt and Crawford's villainy. He did not deserve a wife who despised him, however much his limitations made that contempt understandable.

In the end, Rushworth disappears from the novel, his marriage annulled, his humiliation complete. He is last mentioned as having survived the scandal tolerably well—his money, as always, protecting him from the worst consequences. For a character defined entirely by wealth, this seems fitting: money remains his only resource.
