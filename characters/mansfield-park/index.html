Mansfield Park

Characters from Austen's Most Moral Novel (1814)

Mansfield Park presents Austen's most complex examination of morality, featuring the quiet Fanny Price against the worldly Crawfords. The novel explores themes of integrity, temptation, and the corrupting influence of wealth through a rich cast of flawed and fascinating characters.

Main Characters

Protagonist

Fanny Price

The poor relation brought to Mansfield Park as a child, Fanny is timid, principled, and unfailingly moral. Her quiet strength and constancy are tested by temptation and rejection.

Principled Timid Constant
Hero

Edmund Bertram

The younger son destined for the church, Edmund is Fanny's champion and closest friend. His infatuation with Mary Crawford tests his principles and Fanny's patience.

Honorable Kind Sometimes blind
Antagonist

Mary Crawford

Witty, worldly, and charming, Mary captivates Edmund despite her cynical views on marriage, money, and the church. Her moral bankruptcy is gradually revealed.

Witty Worldly Cynical
Antagonist

Henry Crawford

The Bertram Family & Others

Family

Sir Thomas Bertram

The stern patriarch of Mansfield Park, whose long absence in Antigua allows disorder to flourish. His parenting style proves ineffective.

Authoritarian Well-meaning Distant
Family

Lady Bertram

Sir Thomas's indolent wife, whose languid existence on the sofa represents moral inertia. She loves Fanny but expends no effort on anyone.

Indolent Passive Self-absorbed
Family

Maria Bertram

The eldest Bertram daughter who marries for money, then ruins herself through an affair with Henry Crawford. Pride and passion lead to her downfall.

Proud Passionate Reckless
Family

Julia Bertram

The younger Bertram sister, less proud than Maria but equally ill-educated. Her elopement with Mr. Yates adds to the family's disgrace.

Impulsive Jealous
Family

Tom Bertram

The dissolute eldest son whose extravagance and illness create both financial and emotional crises for the family.

Extravagant Thoughtless
Antagonist

Mrs. Norris

Fanny's malicious aunt who delights in tormenting her while spoiling the Bertram children. One of Austen's most memorable villains.

Cruel Miserly Meddling
Supporting

Mr. Rushworth

Maria's wealthy but foolish husband, whose twelve thousand a year cannot compensate for his stupidity. His marriage ends in disaster.

Wealthy Stupid
Supporting

Mr. Yates

Tom's theatre-mad friend whose arrival catalyzes the Lovers' Vows disaster. His eventual elopement with Julia adds to the family's disgrace.

Theatre-obsessed Foolish
