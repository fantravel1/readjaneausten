Persuasion

Companion Reading Guide

This companion guide provides essential context for reading Persuasion. Understanding the Royal Navy's role in Regency society, the aristocracy's financial pressures, and the novel's complex timeline will deepen your appreciation of Austen's final completed work.

Historical Context: The Navy After the Napoleonic Wars

The Royal Navy in Austen's Time

Persuasion is unique among Austen's novels for its celebration of the Royal Navy. Written during and after the Napoleonic Wars (1803-1815), the novel reflects the Navy's elevated status in British society and its role as a vehicle for social mobility.

Key historical points:

  • The British Navy secured victory at Trafalgar (1805) and dominated the seas throughout the Napoleonic Wars
  • Naval service offered men of modest means the chance to rise through merit and achievement
  • The prize money system could make officers spectacularly wealthy overnight
  • Post-war, many officers like Wentworth found themselves on half-pay with uncertain prospects

The Prize Money System

Captain Wentworth's fortune of ¬£25,000 came from "prize money"‚Äîthe proceeds from captured enemy ships. When a British naval vessel captured an enemy ship and its cargo, the value was assessed and divided among the crew according to rank.

The distribution typically worked as follows:

  • The captain received 3/8 of the total prize value
  • Admirals and commanders-in-chief received shares even if not present at the capture
  • Officers received smaller shares based on rank
  • Common seamen received the smallest shares

A single major capture could make a captain wealthy. Wentworth's ¬£25,000 represents approximately ¬£2-2.5 million in today's money‚Äîa fortune built on skill, courage, and luck, not inheritance.

Naval Ranks Referenced

Admiral Croft: A high-ranking officer who has reached the top of the naval hierarchy. Admirals commanded fleets and were treated as gentlemen of the highest order.

Captain Wentworth: Commander of a ship, the rank most associated with prize money opportunities. A "post captain" like Wentworth commanded larger vessels.

Captain Benwick: Another post captain, though mourning his fianc√©e and thus more melancholy than the active Wentworth.

Captain Harville: A captain injured in service, living modestly on half-pay in Lyme Regis.

Understanding the Elliot Family's Financial Decline

Sir Walter's Predicament

Sir Walter Elliot is a baronet‚Äîa hereditary title ranking below the peerage but above gentry. However, his status brings more vanity than actual wealth. His financial troubles stem from:

  • Living beyond his means to maintain appearances appropriate to his rank
  • Inability to economize without admitting his financial situation
  • No ability to increase his income (land produces fixed rents; he cannot "work")
  • Daughters who need expensive "coming out" seasons and suitable maintenance

The solution‚Äîletting Kellynch Hall and moving to Bath‚Äîrepresents a humiliating descent in status, though Sir Walter manages to delude himself about it.

Anne's Portion

Anne has no fortune of her own. As Sir Walter's younger daughter, she would receive a marriage portion only when (if) she married, and that portion would be modest given the family's financial troubles. This makes her extremely vulnerable‚Äîat twenty-seven, past her "bloom," with no fortune, she has almost no marriage prospects beyond Mr. Elliot's calculated courtship.

Essential Vocabulary

Baronet
A hereditary title (indicated by "Sir") ranking below barons but above knights. Baronets are not peers and cannot sit in the House of Lords, but they pride themselves on their ancient lineages. Sir Walter's obsession with the Baronetage reflects his fixation on rank.
Living Well
In Regency terms, "living well" meant maintaining an establishment appropriate to one's rank‚Äîproper servants, carriages, entertaining, and appearance. Sir Walter's need to "live well" drives his financial ruin.
Being Useful
Anne's desire to be "useful" reflects a moral value opposed to Sir Walter's useless vanity. Being useful meant actively helping others, managing households competently, and contributing to society‚Äîvalues the Navy men embody.
Constancy
Steadfastness and faithfulness in affection. Anne's constancy to Wentworth despite eight years of separation is the novel's moral center. The question is whether constancy is a virtue or a self-destructive refusal to move forward.
Bloom
The freshness and beauty of youth. Anne has lost her "bloom" at twenty-seven‚Äîa devastating judgment in a society that prized youth in women. Her renewed bloom in Bath signals returning hope and happiness.
Letting / Leasing
Renting out property. Sir Walter must let Kellynch Hall to pay his debts‚Äîa humiliating admission of financial failure, though he tries to maintain dignity by choosing a respectable tenant (Admiral Croft).
Half-Pay
Reduced income for naval officers not actively serving. After the war, many officers like Captain Benwick lived on half-pay, creating financial uncertainty for military families.
Coming Out
A young woman's formal introduction to society as eligible for marriage. Anne's "coming out" season eight years ago was when she met and fell in love with Wentworth.

Character Relationships

The Elliot Family

Sir Walter Elliot
Vain, foolish baronet obsessed with rank and appearance. Neglects Anne while doting on Elizabeth. His financial improvidence forces the family to let Kellynch Hall.
Elizabeth Elliot
Sir Walter's eldest daughter and favorite, twenty-nine and unmarried. Proud, cold, and dismissive of Anne. Manages Kellynch with Mrs. Clay's dubious assistance.
Anne Elliot
The protagonist; twenty-seven, faded, unappreciated by her family. Persuaded to break her engagement eight years ago; has regretted it ever since. Kind, competent, constant in her affections.
Mary Musgrove (n√©e Elliot)
Anne's youngest sister; married to Charles Musgrove. Hypochondriacal, complaining, but less vain than Elizabeth. Lives at Uppercross Cottage.
Mr. William Elliot
Sir Walter's heir (as male cousin). Estranged from family for years; suddenly reappears and courts Anne. Charming but secretly selfish and calculating.

The Navy Circle

Captain Frederick Wentworth
Anne's former fianc√©; now wealthy from prize money. Confident, successful, but still angry about Anne's rejection eight years ago. Brother to Mrs. Croft.
Admiral Croft
Rents Kellynch Hall; bluff, kind naval officer. Represents the Navy's merit-based values. Married to Wentworth's sister.
Mrs. Sophia Croft
Admiral's wife and Wentworth's beloved sister. Intelligent, direct, and capable. Has accompanied her husband on voyages. Represents companionate marriage.
Captain Benwick
Melancholy captain mourning his fianc√©e (who died while he was at sea). Reads poetry; talks to Anne about grief. Later marries Louisa Musgrove.
Captain Harville
Injured captain living in Lyme; kind, devoted to friends. His conversation with Anne about constancy prompts Wentworth's letter.

The Musgrove Family

Charles Musgrove
Mary's husband; heir to Uppercross estate. Once proposed to Anne before marrying Mary. Good-natured but somewhat weak.
Mr. & Mrs. Musgrove (Senior)
Hospitable country gentry living at the Great House. Less refined than the Elliots but happier and more genuine.
Henrietta Musgrove
Younger Musgrove daughter; attracted to Wentworth but ultimately returns to her cousin Charles Hayter.
Louisa Musgrove
The more determined Musgrove daughter. Her fall at Lyme is the novel's crisis. Her recovery and engagement to Benwick frees Wentworth emotionally.

Other Key Characters

Lady Russell
Anne's godmother and mother figure. Persuaded Anne to break her engagement eight years ago. Genuinely cares for Anne but has conventional values about rank and propriety.
Mrs. Smith
Anne's old school friend, now widowed and impoverished. Lives in Bath; reveals the truth about Mr. Elliot. Represents loyalty across class lines.
Mrs. Clay
Elizabeth's "friend" and companion; scheming to marry Sir Walter despite being socially beneath him. Mr. Elliot fears she'll produce an heir.

Key Locations

Kellynch Hall
The Elliot family seat in Somersetshire. A handsome estate that Sir Walter can no longer afford to maintain. Letting it to Admiral Croft symbolizes the Navy's rising status and the aristocracy's decline.
Uppercross
The Musgrove estate near Kellynch. Includes the Great House (Mr. and Mrs. Musgrove senior) and the Cottage (Charles and Mary). Represents comfortable, unpretentious country gentry life.
Lyme Regis
Seaside town in Dorset where Captain Harville lives and where Louisa has her accident on the Cobb (the harbor wall). The dramatic coastal setting mirrors the novel's emotional intensity.
Bath
Fashionable spa city where the Elliots relocate to economize (ironically). Bath represents vanity, social pretension, and the marriage market. Anne finds it stifling until Wentworth arrives.
Camden Place
The Elliots' prestigious address in Bath. Sir Walter takes pride in this fashionable location, showing his continued focus on appearances despite his financial troubles.

Novel Timeline: Eight Years Ago vs. Present

Understanding the Eight-Year Gap

The novel's emotional weight depends on understanding what happened eight years before the story begins:

Summer 1806
Anne (age 19) meets Frederick Wentworth (a commander with no fortune) and they fall in love during her "coming out" season
Autumn 1806
They become engaged, but Lady Russell persuades Anne that the match is imprudent‚Äîhe has no fortune, uncertain prospects, and Anne is too young
Winter 1806
Anne breaks off the engagement; Wentworth leaves, angry and hurt. Anne begins eight years of regret
1806-1814
Wentworth serves in the Napoleonic Wars, makes his fortune through prize money (¬£25,000), rises to Captain. Anne fades, loses her bloom, remains unmarried and unappreciated
Summer 1814
THE NOVEL BEGINS: Sir Walter must let Kellynch; Admiral Croft (Wentworth's brother-in-law) becomes the tenant

Present Timeline (1814)

Late Summer
Anne (now 27) stays with Mary at Uppercross; Wentworth arrives to visit his sister at Kellynch; painful reunion
Early Autumn
Wentworth spends time with the Musgroves; seems to court Louisa; the trip to Lyme; Louisa's fall from the Cobb
November
Anne goes to Bath; Mr. Elliot begins his courtship; Louisa's engagement to Benwick is announced
December
Mrs. Smith reveals Mr. Elliot's true character; Wentworth comes to Bath; the concert
Late December
Wentworth writes his passionate letter; Anne and Wentworth are engaged; the novel ends with their marriage assured
