Emma

Companion Reading Guide

This companion guide provides essential context for reading Emma. Understanding the social dynamics of a small English village, the subtle gradations of class, and Austen's sophisticated irony will deepen your appreciation of this masterpiece.

Historical Context

Genteel Country Society

Published in 1815, Emma is set in the years leading up to its publication, though the exact timeframe remains deliberately vague. Unlike Austen's other novels, which feature travel to London, Bath, or the seaside, Emma takes place entirely within the confined world of Highbury, a fictional village sixteen miles from London.

This setting allows Austen to examine in microscopic detail the social hierarchies and relationships of a small community where everyone knows everyone else's business, fortunes, and family connections.

Key aspects of this world:

  • Social position was determined by birth, property ownership, and connections
  • The gentry maintained careful distinctions between themselves and tradespeople
  • Women's options were severely limited‚Äîmarriage, dependency, or genteel poverty
  • Small communities operated on strict codes of visiting, etiquette, and propriety

The Status of Governesses

Jane Fairfax's impending career as a governess is treated as a kind of genteel slavery. Governesses occupied an ambiguous position‚Äîtoo educated to associate with servants, but employed and thus not equal to the family. They faced isolation, dependency, and often harsh treatment. For a talented, educated woman like Jane, it represents a tragic waste of potential and a loss of autonomy.

Understanding Highbury's Social Hierarchy

The novel's conflicts arise largely from the fine gradations of rank in Highbury. Understanding who stands where clarifies Emma's snobbery and the social risks various characters take.

The Significance of "Elegance of Mind"

When Emma tells Harriet that Mr. Elton requires a woman with "elegance of mind," she's making a crucial class distinction. "Elegance of mind" refers to refined manners, education, taste, and social graces‚Äîqualities supposedly found only in the gentry. Emma wrongly assumes Harriet possesses these qualities, while dismissing the worthy Robert Martin as lacking them. Austen's irony shows that "elegance of mind" has little to do with birth and much to do with genuine goodness and sense.

Essential Vocabulary

Handsome
The famous opening describes Emma as "handsome, clever, and rich." In Regency usage, "handsome" meant attractive with dignity and good features, though not necessarily beautiful in a delicate way.
Tolerable
When applied to people or situations, means "acceptable" or "passable" rather than excellent. Emma finds Robert Martin "tolerable" but not genteel enough for Harriet.
Elegance of Mind
Refined taste, manners, education, and sensibility supposedly found in the gentry class. Emma uses this as justification for class prejudice.
Coming Out
A young woman's formal entry into society as eligible for marriage. Emma has "come out" and presides over Highbury society.
Natural Daughter
A euphemism for illegitimate child. Harriet is someone's "natural daughter," though her parentage is initially unknown.
Parlour Boarder
A student at a school who pays extra to live with the school mistress's family rather than in dormitories‚Äîa sign of higher status. Harriet is a parlour boarder at Mrs. Goddard's.
Governess
An educated woman employed to teach children in a private home. The only respectable employment for gentlewomen, but considered a form of "degradation" requiring total dependency.
Exploring Party
An excursion or outing, like the trips to Box Hill or Donwell Abbey. These occasions allowed for socializing outside formal calls.

Character Relationships

Emma's Social Web

Understanding the complex relationships in Highbury is essential to following the plot:

Emma & Mr. Woodhouse
Emma's valetudinarian father depends on her completely. His hypochondria and resistance to change limit Emma's world and keep her at Hartfield.
Emma & Mr. Knightley
Old family friends. Knightley's brother married Emma's sister. He's 16-17 years older and has known Emma since she was a child. He alone criticizes her honestly.
Emma & Harriet Smith
Emma takes up Harriet as a prot√©g√©e, flattered by her admiration. Their friendship is marked by inequality‚ÄîEmma directs, Harriet obeys.
Emma & Jane Fairfax
They should be friends‚Äîsimilar age, both accomplished‚Äîbut Emma resents Jane's superiority in accomplishments and dislikes her reserve.
Emma & Miss Bates
Miss Bates is Jane's aunt and a harmless, talkative spinster whom Emma finds tedious. Emma's treatment of her reveals her capacity for unkindness.
Emma & Mrs. Weston
Emma's former governess and closest friend. Mrs. Weston is too indulgent to check Emma's faults, having raised her with excessive praise.

The Secret Engagement

Frank Churchill and Jane Fairfax's secret engagement is the novel's central mystery. Understanding why they hide it explains much of the plot:

Why Secret?
Frank depends entirely on his aunt Mrs. Churchill's goodwill for his inheritance. She would never approve his marrying a penniless nobody like Jane.
Frank's Behavior
Frank flirts with Emma to disguise his true attachment. His apparent attentions to Emma are a screen, though they're genuine enough to mislead everyone.
Jane's Reserve
Jane must hide her feelings and endure seeing Frank flirt with Emma. Her coldness is the strain of living a lie and knowing she faces dependence as a governess.
The Pianoforte
Frank's secret gift to Jane. Emma assumes it's from Mr. Dixon (Jane's friend's husband), revealing her capacity for unkind speculation.

Key Locations

Hartfield
The Woodhouse estate. Though less grand than Donwell Abbey, it's one of Highbury's principal properties. Emma is its mistress and reigns over local society from here.
Donwell Abbey
Mr. Knightley's ancestral estate with its ancient abbey ruins. Represents tradition, stability, and proper English values. The strawberry party there is a crucial scene.
Randalls
The Westons' home, purchased with Mr. Weston's earnings from trade. Smaller than Hartfield or Donwell but respectable. Frank Churchill visits here.
Box Hill
A local beauty spot where the characters go for an exploring party. The scene of Emma's famous insult to Miss Bates and Mr. Knightley's rebuke.
Mrs. Goddard's School
A boarding school for girls where Harriet Smith was educated as a parlour boarder. Represents Harriet's uncertain social position.
Abbey Mill Farm
Robert Martin's prosperous farm, rented from Mr. Knightley's estate. A substantial property, though Martin is a farmer, not gentry.
Ford's
The principal shop in Highbury where the characters purchase gloves, fabric, and other goods. A gathering place for the community.

Novel Timeline

Autumn (Year 1)
Miss Taylor marries Mr. Weston and becomes Mrs. Weston. Emma befriends Harriet Smith and discourages her attachment to Robert Martin.
October
Emma begins her matchmaking scheme between Harriet and Mr. Elton. Robert Martin proposes to Harriet; Emma convinces her to refuse.
December
Mr. Elton proposes to Emma (not Harriet) after the Christmas Eve party. Emma realizes her terrible mistake.
January (Year 2)
Mr. Elton goes to Bath and soon becomes engaged to Augusta Hawkins, a woman with ¬£10,000.
February
Frank Churchill's long-anticipated visit is postponed due to Mrs. Churchill's illness. Jane Fairfax returns to Highbury.
Late February
Frank Churchill finally arrives. The mysterious pianoforte is delivered to Jane Fairfax. Emma and Frank begin their flirtation.
April
The Eltons return to Highbury as a married couple. Mrs. Elton immediately irritates everyone with her presumption.
June
Strawberry party at Donwell Abbey. Emma observes tensions between Frank and Jane. The disastrous Box Hill excursion; Emma insults Miss Bates.
Late June
Mr. Knightley rebukes Emma. Mrs. Churchill dies. Frank and Jane's engagement is revealed. Emma realizes she loves Mr. Knightley.
July
Mr. Knightley proposes to Emma. Harriet becomes engaged to Robert Martin (with Emma's blessing). Double weddings planned.
October
The novel concludes with the marriages of Frank-Jane and Emma-Mr. Knightley, followed soon by Harriet-Robert Martin's wedding.
