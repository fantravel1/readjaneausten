Northanger Abbey

Companion Reading Guide

This companion guide provides essential context for reading Northanger Abbey. Understanding Gothic novel conventions, Bath society, and the literary culture of the 1790s will enrich your appreciation of Austen's brilliant satire and social commentary.

Historical Context: The Gothic Novel Craze

The Gothic Novel (1760s-1820s)

Gothic novels dominated popular fiction in the late 18th century, especially among female readers. These sensational stories featured:

  • Mysterious castles, abbeys, and convents with secret passages
  • Innocent heroines threatened by sinister male figures
  • Supernatural elements (or seemingly supernatural events later explained)
  • Mediterranean settings (Italy, France, Spain)
  • Atmosphere of terror, suspense, and melodrama
  • Family secrets, missing heirs, and hidden documents

The Gothic novel was immensely popular but also controversial. Critics condemned them as dangerous, sensational trash that corrupted young women's minds. Austen engages directly with this debate.

Ann Radcliffe: Queen of Gothic Romance

Ann Radcliffe (1764-1823) was the most celebrated Gothic novelist of her era. Her works, especially The Mysteries of Udolpho (1794), defined the genre. Radcliffe's innovation was the "explained supernatural"‚Äîseemingly ghostly events always have rational explanations.

Catherine Morland is obsessed with Radcliffe's novels. Understanding Radcliffe's conventions helps readers appreciate Austen's parody. In Radcliffe's novels:

  • Virtuous heroines are imprisoned in gloomy castles
  • Mysterious sounds and sights create atmospheric terror
  • Evil guardians threaten heroines' virtue and fortune
  • Landscape descriptions emphasize the sublime and beautiful
  • Everything is eventually explained rationally (no actual ghosts)

Why Northanger Abbey?

Austen wrote Northanger Abbey (originally titled Susan) between 1798-1803, at the height of Gothic novel popularity. By the time it was published in 1817, the Gothic craze had waned, making some of Austen's satire less immediately topical. However, her broader themes‚Äîabout reading critically, distinguishing fantasy from reality, and recognizing true versus artificial dangers‚Äîremain timeless.

Bath as a Social Setting

Georgian Bath: The Marriage Market

Bath was the premier resort town in Georgian England, famous for its Roman baths, elegant architecture, and social scene. The "Season" in Bath (autumn and winter) was where fashionable society gathered to:

  • Take the healthful waters for real or imagined ailments
  • Attend assembly room balls and card parties
  • Promenade and be seen in the Pump Room and on public walks
  • Shop in the elegant boutiques on Milsom Street
  • Most importantly: meet potential marriage partners

Bath's social rituals were highly structured. The Master of Ceremonies enforced etiquette rules, morning visits had strict protocols, and public assemblies provided supervised opportunities for young people to meet. For Catherine, arriving from rural Wiltshire, Bath represents excitement, sophistication, and social possibility.

Key Bath Locations in the Novel

  • The Pump Room: Social hub where visitors drank the mineral water and socialized in the mornings
  • The Upper Rooms: Assembly rooms for balls and card parties (where Catherine first sees Henry Tilney)
  • The Lower Rooms: Another assembly venue, considered slightly less fashionable
  • Milsom Street: Fashionable shopping street where Catherine walks with the Tilneys
  • Beechen Cliff: Hill outside Bath where Catherine walks with the Tilneys (Henry's picturesque lecture)

Essential Vocabulary

Horrid
In Gothic parlance, "horrid" meant thrilling, terrifying, sublime‚Äînot necessarily bad. Isabella's "horrid novels" recommendation is enthusiastic, not critical. The word appears throughout to signal Gothic sensibility.
Sensible
Having good sense, rational, judicious. Not primarily about emotions. Henry describes Eleanor as "sensible," meaning she has sound judgment. Catherine must become more "sensible."
Gothic
Originally referring to medieval architecture (pointed arches, etc.), by Austen's time meant anything medieval, mysterious, or associated with Gothic novels‚Äîcastles, abbeys, romantic ruins.
The Abbey
Northanger Abbey, though called an "abbey," is a converted monastery‚Äîdissolved under Henry VIII‚Äînow a grand country house. Many Gothic novels featured abbeys and convents, making this setting charged with literary associations.
Picturesque
An aesthetic category (along with the Beautiful and Sublime) describing landscape views that would make good pictures‚Äîrugged, irregular, artfully wild. Henry lectures Catherine on picturesque principles.
The Season
The period when fashionable society congregated in Bath (autumn/winter) or London (spring). Timing determined social opportunities and marriage prospects.
Assembly
A public ball held at assembly rooms, where people could meet and dance under supervised conditions. Assemblies had strict etiquette rules about introductions and dancing partners.
Gig / Curricle
Light, two-wheeled carriages. John Thorpe constantly brags about his "gig." A curricle (which Henry Tilney drives) was sportier and more fashionable‚Äîa status symbol.

Catherine's Gothic Reading List

In Chapter 6, Isabella Thorpe recommends seven "horrid novels" to Catherine. These were real Gothic novels popular in the 1790s, mostly published by the Minerva Press, which specialized in sensational fiction. Here's what Catherine (and you) might read:

The Mysteries of Udolpho
by Ann Radcliffe (1794)
The quintessential Gothic novel. Heroine Emily St. Aubert is imprisoned in Castle Udolpho by the sinister Montoni. Features mysterious veils, secret passages, apparently supernatural events (all rationally explained), and sublime Italian scenery. This is the novel that most influences Catherine's imagination.
The Italian, or The Confessional of the Black Penitents
by Ann Radcliffe (1797)
Radcliffe's other major novel. Features the terrifying monk Schedoni, the Italian Inquisition, and a imprisoned heroine. Darker and more suspenseful than Udolpho.
Castle of Wolfenbach
by Eliza Parsons (1793)
One of Isabella's "horrid novels." Features a persecuted countess, a haunted castle in Germany, and family secrets. Typical Minerva Press Gothic fare.
Clermont
by Regina Maria Roche (1798)
Another of the "horrid" seven. Involves an orphaned heroine, a wicked guardian, and a mysterious castle. Very popular in its day.
The Mysterious Warning
by Eliza Parsons (1796)
Isabella's third recommendation. Features ominous warnings, family curses, and gothic atmosphere.
The Necromancer; or, The Tale of the Black Forest
by Karl Friedrich Kahlert (translated 1794)
A German Gothic tale translated into English. Features necromancy, the Black Forest, and supernatural horror.
The Midnight Bell
by Francis Lathom (1798)
Involves a mysterious bell that tolls at midnight, predicting deaths. Gothic suspense at its most sensational.
Orphan of the Rhine
by Eleanor Sleath (1798)
The seventh "horrid" novel. Features an orphaned heroine, a German castle, and standard Gothic persecution.
Horrid Mysteries
by Carl Grosse (translated 1796)
The final recommendation. A German Gothic involving secret societies, mysterious conspiracies, and sensational plotting.

Character Relationships

The Morland Family

Catherine Morland
Protagonist, age 17; ordinary and unaccomplished but good-natured; obsessed with Gothic novels; must learn to read both books and people critically.
Reverend Richard Morland
Catherine's father; country clergyman with comfortable income; sensible and unimaginative‚Äîthe opposite of Gothic.
James Morland
Catherine's eldest brother, at Oxford; falls for Isabella Thorpe; learns painful lessons about her mercenary character.

The Tilney Family

Henry Tilney
Clever, witty clergyman, about 24-25; teases and educates Catherine; loves Gothic novels ironically while Catherine loves them literally.
Eleanor Tilney
Henry's sister; kind, sensible, and gentle; becomes Catherine's true friend (contrasting with false friend Isabella); lives under her father's domination.
General Tilney
Widowed father; stern and controlling; obsessed with money and status; the novel's real villain‚Äînot Gothic but realistically mercenary.
Captain Frederick Tilney
Henry's elder brother, a military officer; flirts with Isabella to amuse himself, exposing her inconstancy.

The Thorpe Family

Isabella Thorpe
Beautiful, manipulative fortune-hunter; pretends friendship with Catherine while pursuing her brother James; shifts affections to wealthier Captain Tilney.
John Thorpe
James Morland's Oxford friend; boorish, boastful, and dishonest; pursues Catherine; his lies to General Tilney drive the plot's crisis.
Mrs. Thorpe
Widow with limited means; old school friend of Mrs. Allen; her children have learned her fortune-hunting ways.

The Allen Family

Mr. Allen
Wealthy, gouty gentleman who brings his wife to Bath for the waters; Catherine's neighbor and patron.
Mrs. Allen
Kind but vapid; obsessed with fashion and muslins; inadequate as a chaperone or guide for Catherine; provides no real wisdom.

Key Locations

Fullerton
The Morland family home in Wiltshire. A comfortable country parsonage representing ordinary, ungothic English life‚Äîthe reality Catherine must learn to appreciate.
Bath, Somerset
Fashionable spa town where Catherine experiences society, makes friends (true and false), and meets Henry Tilney. Represents worldly education and social complexity.
Northanger Abbey, Gloucestershire
The Tilney family seat, a converted medieval abbey. Catherine expects Gothic horrors but finds modern comforts‚Äîthough the real villain (General Tilney) proves more dangerous than imaginary ghosts.
Woodston
Henry Tilney's parsonage, a new and comfortable house. Represents domestic happiness and realistic expectations‚Äîwhat Catherine's future with Henry will actually look like.
Blaize Castle
John Thorpe's planned excursion (Catherine misses her walk with the Tilneys for this). Not actually a medieval castle but an 18th-century folly‚Äîa fake Gothic building, much like the fake friendship of the Thorpes.

Novel Timeline

Winter/Early Spring
Catherine arrives in Bath with the Allens; meets Henry Tilney at the Upper Rooms assembly
Week 2-3
Catherine befriends Isabella Thorpe; her brother James arrives; John Thorpe pursues Catherine
Week 4-5
Catherine reencounters Henry and meets Eleanor Tilney; social conflicts with the Thorpes escalate
Week 6
James proposes to Isabella (she accepts); Catherine invited to Northanger Abbey; they leave Bath
Week 7-8
At Northanger: Catherine explores the Abbey, fantasizes about Mrs. Tilney's death, discovers the truth
Week 9
Henry rebukes Catherine's Gothic imaginings; she begins to see clearly; visit to Woodston parsonage
Week 9-10
News of Isabella's inconstancy arrives; General Tilney suddenly expels Catherine from Northanger
Week 10-11
Catherine returns home in disgrace; Henry follows to propose; General Tilney's motivations revealed
Several Months Later
General Tilney relents; Catherine and Henry marry; Eleanor makes a brilliant match

Key Passages to Watch

"The person, be it gentleman or lady, who has not pleasure in a good novel, must be intolerably stupid."

‚Äî Narrator, Chapter 5

Austen's famous defense of novel-reading addresses critics who dismissed fiction as frivolous. She argues for novels as serious art worthy of intelligent readers' attention.

"Charming as were all Mrs. Radcliffe's works, and charming even as were the works of all her imitators, it was not in them perhaps that human nature, at least in the midland counties of England, was to be looked for."

‚Äî Narrator, Chapter 24

After Henry rebukes Catherine, the narrator gently reminds us that Gothic conventions don't apply to English country life‚Äîreal human nature is more mundane but also more complex.

"If I understand you rightly, you had formed a surmise of such horror as I have hardly words to‚Äî Dear Miss Morland, consider the dreadful nature of the suspicions you have entertained."

‚Äî Henry Tilney, Chapter 24

Henry's rebuke when he discovers Catherine suspects his father of murdering his mother. This moment shatters Catherine's Gothic fantasies and teaches her to distinguish fiction from reality.

"Catherine, at any rate, heard enough to feel that in suspecting General Tilney of either murdering or shutting up his wife, she had scarcely sinned against his character, or magnified his cruelty."

‚Äî Narrator, Chapter 30

The novel's brilliant reversal: Catherine was wrong about Gothic horrors, but right to sense something wrong with General Tilney. His mercenary cruelty is real, if not supernatural.
