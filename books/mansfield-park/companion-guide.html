Mansfield Park

Companion Reading Guide

This companion guide provides essential context for reading Mansfield Park. Understanding the historical background of slavery and the slave trade, the role of the clergy, naval careers, and Regency-era social structures will deepen your appreciation of Austen's most morally serious novel.

Historical Context

The Slave Trade and Antigua

Sir Thomas Bertram's wealth comes from a sugar plantation in Antigua, worked by enslaved people. This is one of the earliest references in English literature to acknowledge the colonial and slave-labor foundations of English domestic comfort.

Key historical context:

  • The British slave trade was abolished in 1807, but slavery itself continued in British colonies until 1833
  • Sugar plantations in the Caribbean were brutally profitable enterprises built on slave labor
  • Many English gentry families owed their wealth to colonial plantations
  • Fanny's question about the slave trade and the resulting "dead silence" suggests both awareness and complicity
  • Austen's own family connections (godfather owned plantations) make this reference particularly significant

The Clergy and "Taking Orders"

Edmund's decision to take orders (become ordained as a clergyman) is treated as a serious moral commitment, not merely a career choice. This reflects the period's understanding of clergy as moral authorities.

Understanding the clergy:

  • A "living" was a parish appointment providing income, housing (the parsonage), and social standing
  • Livings were often in the gift of wealthy landowners who could bestow them on younger sons
  • Clergymen were gentlemen expected to provide moral leadership and practical charity
  • Mary Crawford's mockery of the clergy as a profession reveals her worldliness
  • The contrast between Dr. Grant (indulgent) and Edmund (principled) shows varieties of clerical life

Naval Careers and the Price Family

Fanny's beloved brother William serves in the Royal Navy during the Napoleonic Wars. Naval service offered one of the few paths to advancement for men without fortune.

Naval context:

  • The navy offered meritocratic advancement through prize money from captured ships
  • A lieutenant's commission (which William receives) was a crucial step toward prosperity
  • Naval officers could rise from poverty to wealth through bravery and luck
  • The wars with France (1793-1815) made naval careers both dangerous and potentially lucrative
  • Austen's own brothers served in the navy, making her naval references particularly authentic

Understanding the Bertram Family Wealth

The novel's moral universe is complicated by the colonial origins of Mansfield Park's prosperity. Sir Thomas must travel to Antigua to address problems with his plantation‚Äîthe source of the family's ¬£4,000 per year.

The Economics of Mansfield Park

Unlike Pride and Prejudice's land-based wealth, Mansfield Park's fortune comes from colonial exploitation. This casts a moral shadow over the entire estate and raises questions about complicity and responsibility.

The novel invites us to consider:

  • How does colonial wealth affect our judgment of the Bertrams?
  • What does Fanny's question about slavery‚Äîand the silence that follows‚Äîtell us?
  • Is Austen critiquing the source of English comfort or merely acknowledging it?
  • How does the Price family's poverty contrast with Bertram wealth built on slavery?

Essential Vocabulary

Living
A clergyman's position, including the parish, parsonage, and income. Edmund will receive the Thornton Lacey living worth ¬£700/year. Livings were often "in the gift" of wealthy patrons.
Taking Orders / Ordination
Being ordained as a clergyman in the Church of England. Edmund's decision to take orders represents a serious moral commitment to duty and service.
Coming Out
A young woman's formal introduction to society as eligible for marriage. Fanny's "coming out" ball marks her transition from child to marriageable woman.
Improvement
Landscape design and estate modernization, but also moral development. The Sotherton visit raises questions about what kind of "improvement" matters most.
Private Theatricals
Amateur dramatic performances in private homes. Controversial because acting allowed people to step outside social boundaries and express forbidden feelings.
Presentation at Court
Formal introduction of a young woman to the monarch, marking her official entry into high society. Maria and Julia are presented; Fanny is not.
Rears and Vices
Mary Crawford's inappropriate joke about naval ranks (Rear Admirals and Vice Admirals), revealing her worldliness and lack of proper decorum.
Ha-ha
A sunken fence creating a boundary without blocking the view‚Äîused in landscape gardening. Featured in the Sotherton visit discussions.

Character Relationships

The Bertram Family

Sir Thomas Bertram
Patriarch; owner of Mansfield Park and Antigua plantation; dignified but distant father; wealth derived from colonial slavery.
Lady Bertram
Indolent mother; spends days on sofa with pug; delegates parenting to others; beautiful but passive.
Mrs. Norris
Lady Bertram's sister; widow; interfering, mean-spirited, plays favorites; brought Fanny to Mansfield but torments her.
Tom Bertram (eldest son)
Heir to Mansfield; irresponsible, gambles, organizes the theatricals; serious illness later reforms him.
Edmund Bertram (second son)
Principled younger son; becomes clergyman; Fanny's teacher and eventual husband; temporarily blinded by Mary Crawford.
Maria Bertram
Beautiful eldest daughter; marries wealthy Mr. Rushworth despite preferring Henry Crawford; later commits adultery.
Julia Bertram
Second daughter; also attracted to Henry; elopes with Mr. Yates; lesser scandal than Maria's.

The Price Family

Frances Price (Mrs. Price)
Fanny's mother; Lady Bertram's sister; married imprudently to naval lieutenant; overwhelmed by poverty and nine children.
Fanny Price
Protagonist; sent to Mansfield at 10; poor relation; morally steadfast; loves Edmund; refuses Henry Crawford despite pressure.
William Price
Fanny's beloved brother; naval midshipman then lieutenant; visits Mansfield; represents meritocratic advancement.
Susan Price
Fanny's younger sister; intelligent and eager; eventually comes to Mansfield; shows Fanny's Portsmouth connections needn't be shameful.

The Crawford Family

Mary Crawford
Charming, witty, worldly; raised by dissolute uncle; loves Edmund but cannot accept his clerical calling; fails moral test.
Henry Crawford
Charming fortune hunter; flirts with both Bertram sisters; falls genuinely for Fanny; moral collapse with Maria ends his hopes.
Admiral Crawford
Deceased uncle who raised Mary and Henry; kept a mistress; his immoral household shaped the Crawfords' worldly values.

Other Key Characters

Dr. Grant
Clergyman at Mansfield parsonage; Mary and Henry's brother-in-law; self-indulgent; represents clergy without true vocation.
Mrs. Grant
Dr. Grant's wife; Mary Crawford's half-sister; kind and sociable; facilitates social gatherings.
Mr. Rushworth
Wealthy but foolish owner of Sotherton; marries Maria despite her indifference; eventually divorces her after her adultery.
Mr. Yates
Friend of Tom's; brings the idea of theatricals to Mansfield; eventually elopes with Julia Bertram.

Key Locations

Mansfield Park
The Bertram family estate in Northamptonshire. A grand establishment representing order, tradition, and (problematically) colonial wealth. Fanny's spiritual home despite her poor relation status.
Portsmouth
The Price family's cramped, noisy, chaotic home. Naval port city. Fanny's return visit shows her how much she's changed and deepens her appreciation for Mansfield's order.
Thornton Lacey
The clerical living Edmund will receive. Mary Crawford wants him to "improve" it into a gentleman's residence, revealing her misunderstanding of clerical duties.
The Parsonage
Dr. and Mrs. Grant's home at Mansfield, where the Crawfords stay. Site of social gatherings, card parties, and much of the Crawford-Bertram courtship.
Sotherton
Mr. Rushworth's grand but old-fashioned estate. The visit reveals character through responses to "improvement" and contains the symbolic wilderness walk.
The East Room
Fanny's small room at Mansfield, originally a schoolroom. Her private refuge containing her books, plants, and writing desk. Represents her marginal position in the household.
Antigua
Sir Thomas's sugar plantation in the West Indies. Never directly depicted but crucial‚Äîthe colonial source of Mansfield's wealth and the site of slavery.

Novel Timeline

Age 10 (Year 1)
Fanny arrives at Mansfield Park from Portsmouth; only Edmund shows her kindness
Ages 10-18
Fanny grows up at Mansfield; educated alongside her cousins but treated as inferior; Edmund becomes her mentor
Age 18 (Autumn)
Sir Thomas leaves for Antigua; the Crawfords arrive at the parsonage; social life intensifies
Winter
Visit to Sotherton; private theatricals planned and rehearsed; Sir Thomas returns unexpectedly; theater dismantled
Early Spring
Maria marries Mr. Rushworth; Henry Crawford leaves; Mary stays; Edmund increasingly attached to Mary
Later Spring
Henry Crawford returns; begins pursuit of Fanny; William visits; Fanny's ball; Henry proposes
Winter/Spring
Fanny refuses Henry; Sir Thomas pressures her; Fanny sent to Portsmouth as punishment/test
Several Months
Fanny in Portsmouth; Henry visits; Edmund wavers on Mary; Tom falls dangerously ill
Spring/Summer
Maria's adultery with Henry Crawford; scandal breaks; Mary's reaction reveals her values; Edmund disillusioned
Resolution
Fanny returns to Mansfield; Edmund recovers from Mary; gradually turns to Fanny; marriage; happiness at Thornton Lacey
