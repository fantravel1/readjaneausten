Pride and Prejudice

Companion Reading Guide

This companion guide provides essential context for reading Pride and Prejudice. Understanding Regency-era customs, vocabulary, and social structures will enrich your experience of Austen's masterpiece.

Historical Context

The Regency Era (1811-1820)

Though Pride and Prejudice was published in 1813, it was begun in 1796-1797 under the title "First Impressions." The novel reflects the social world of the English gentry during this transitional period between Georgian and Victorian eras.

Key historical context:

  • The Napoleonic Wars affected trade and military careers
  • The landed gentry sat between aristocracy and merchant classes
  • Women had very limited legal rights and economic options
  • Marriage was the primary means of women's financial security

The Marriage Market

For genteel women without fortune, marriage was not merely romantic‚Äîit was economic survival. Unmarried women faced limited options: dependence on relatives, becoming a governess, or genteel poverty. This context makes the Bennet sisters' situation urgent, not merely dramatic.

The "Season" in London was the formal marriage market where eligible young women were introduced to society and potential husbands. Country assemblies like the Meryton ball served similar purposes in rural areas.

The Entail

The entail on Longbourn means the estate must pass to the nearest male heir (Mr. Collins) upon Mr. Bennet's death. Mrs. Bennet and her daughters will be left with only the interest from her marriage settlement‚Äîabout ¬£200-250 per year total for six women, forcing dependence on relatives or marriages.

Essential Vocabulary

Entail
Legal restriction on inheritance that prevents property from being divided or sold, typically keeping it in the male line.
Living
A clergyman's position, including the parish, parsonage, and income. Mr. Collins has Lady Catherine's "gift" of a living.
Coming Out
A young woman's formal introduction to society as eligible for marriage, typically at 17-18. Lydia "comes out" at 15, unusually young.
Trade
Commerce or business‚Äîconsidered beneath the gentry. The Gardiners' connection to trade in Cheapside is a mark against them in Miss Bingley's eyes.
Portion / Fortune
A woman's inheritance or dowry, which became her husband's upon marriage. The Bennet daughters' small portions make them less attractive matches.
Connexion
Family relationships or social associations. Darcy worries about Elizabeth's "connexions" affecting his social standing.
Equipage
A carriage with horses and servants‚Äîa status symbol. The type of carriage one kept indicated wealth and rank.
Chaise
A light carriage for traveling. A "chaise and four" (four horses) was faster and more expensive than "chaise and pair."

Character Relationships

The Bennet Family

Mr. Bennet
Patriarch; witty but negligent father; married unwisely for beauty; retreats into sarcasm and his library.
Mrs. Bennet
Mother obsessed with marrying daughters; vulgar but motivated by real economic anxiety about their futures.
Jane (eldest, ~22)
Beautiful, sweet, sees good in everyone; loves Mr. Bingley; too trusting for the world's cynicism.
Elizabeth (~20)
Protagonist; witty, intelligent, proud; her prejudices blind her; learns self-knowledge through the novel.
Mary (middle)
Plain, pedantic; seeks distinction through "accomplishments" and moral pronouncements.
Kitty (~17)
Follower of Lydia; silly but improvable; under Jane and Elizabeth's influence, she later improves.
Lydia (youngest, 15)
Wild, thoughtless, focused on officers and fun; her elopement nearly ruins the family.

Other Key Characters

Fitzwilliam Darcy
Wealthy landowner; proud and reserved; learns to value Elizabeth's honesty over social propriety.
Charles Bingley
Darcy's amiable friend; new wealth from trade; too easily influenced by others; loves Jane.
George Wickham
Charming fortune hunter; son of Darcy's father's steward; manipulative and dishonest.
Mr. Collins
Ridiculous clergyman; heir to Longbourn; obsequious to Lady Catherine; proposes to Elizabeth, marries Charlotte.
Charlotte Lucas
Elizabeth's sensible friend; pragmatic about marriage; accepts Collins for security despite knowing him ridiculous.
Lady Catherine de Bourgh
Darcy's wealthy, domineering aunt; embodies aristocratic arrogance; opposes Darcy-Elizabeth match.

Key Locations

Longbourn
The Bennet family home in Hertfordshire. A modest country house with an estate producing ¬£2,000 per year. Entailed to Mr. Collins.
Netherfield Park
The grand estate Mr. Bingley rents near Longbourn. His tenancy signals he's seeking a permanent home and wife.
Meryton
The nearby market town where the militia is quartered. Site of assemblies, shops, and the Philips' home.
Rosings Park
Lady Catherine de Bourgh's grand estate in Kent. Mr. Collins's parsonage is on the grounds.
Pemberley
Darcy's magnificent estate in Derbyshire. Its beauty and his housekeeper's praise begin Elizabeth's change of heart.
Lambton
The village near Pemberley where Mrs. Gardiner grew up. Elizabeth visits while touring with the Gardiners.

Novel Timeline

Autumn
Bingley takes Netherfield; Meryton assembly; Bingley falls for Jane; Darcy insults Elizabeth
November
Jane's illness at Netherfield; Elizabeth stays to nurse her; verbal sparring with Darcy begins
Late November
Mr. Collins arrives; Wickham appears; Collins proposes to Elizabeth; Netherfield ball
December
Bingley leaves Netherfield; Charlotte accepts Collins; Jane goes to London to visit the Gardiners
March
Elizabeth visits Charlotte at Hunsford; meets Lady Catherine; encounters Darcy
April
Darcy's first proposal; his letter explaining Wickham; Elizabeth's self-revelation
July/August
Elizabeth tours Derbyshire with the Gardiners; visits Pemberley; unexpectedly meets Darcy
August
Lydia's elopement; Darcy secretly arranges the marriage; family crisis
September
Bingley returns to Netherfield; proposes to Jane; Lady Catherine confronts Elizabeth
October
Darcy's second proposal; double wedding of Jane-Bingley and Elizabeth-Darcy
