Sense and Sensibility

Companion Reading Guide

This companion guide provides essential context for reading Sense and Sensibility. Understanding the economic pressures, inheritance laws, and social conventions of Regency England will illuminate the stakes driving this story of two sisters navigating love and loss.

Historical Context

Women's Property Rights & Inheritance

Sense and Sensibility opens with a devastating illustration of how inheritance laws favored male heirs. When Henry Dashwood dies, his estate passes entirely to his son John from a first marriage, despite his wish to provide for his second wife and three daughters.

Key legal realities:

  • Estates were typically entailed to male heirs to keep property intact across generations
  • Married women had no legal right to own property‚Äîit belonged to their husbands
  • Unmarried women depended on fathers, brothers, or male relatives for financial support
  • Women without family support faced limited options: marriage, governessing, or poverty

The Dashwood women's reduction from ¬£4,000 to ¬£500 per year represents a catastrophic loss of status and security, making marriage not merely romantic but essential for survival.

The Culture of Sensibility

In the late 18th century, "sensibility" referred to a prized capacity for refined emotional response, particularly to beauty, nature, and suffering. The cult of sensibility valued:

  • Intense emotional responsiveness and expression
  • Appreciation for the picturesque and sublime in nature
  • Spontaneous feeling over calculated reason
  • Sympathy and compassion as moral virtues

Marianne embodies this cultural ideal, while Austen questions whether unrestrained sensibility serves women well in a society that judges and constrains them. The novel participates in contemporary debates about emotion versus reason, feeling versus propriety.

The Dashwood Women's Crisis

The reduction from ¬£4,000 to ¬£500 per year for four women (Mrs. Dashwood and her three daughters) means they drop from comfortable gentry to genteel poverty. At ¬£125 per person annually, they must economize severely. This makes the sisters' marriage prospects‚Äîand their dowries‚Äîcritically important.

Neither Elinor nor Marianne has a significant fortune to bring to marriage, making them less attractive to fortune-hunting suitors but also making economically secure marriages more urgent.

Essential Vocabulary

Sensibility
Refined emotional responsiveness; capacity for intense feeling, particularly regarding beauty, nature, and moral situations. Marianne embodies Romantic-era sensibility.
Living
A clergyman's position, including the parish, parsonage, and income. Colonel Brandon gives Edward the living at Delaford when he's disinherited.
Constancy
Faithfulness and steadfastness in love or principle. Edward's constancy to his unwise engagement and Brandon's patient devotion exemplify this virtue.
Consumption / Decline
Tuberculosis, then called consumption. Characters suffering from "decline" were wasting away from this disease. Eliza Williams dies from it.
Settled
Financially secured through legal arrangements. Willoughby's debts force him to marry a woman with settled wealth.
Competence
Sufficient income to live comfortably without luxury. Characters debate what income constitutes a "competence."
Natural Daughter
Illegitimate child. Eliza Williams, seduced by Willoughby, is Colonel Brandon's ward and his first love's illegitimate daughter.
Coming Out
A young woman's formal introduction to society as eligible for marriage. Marianne's openness about her feelings for Willoughby before any engagement scandalizes propriety.

Character Relationships

The Dashwood Family

Mrs. Dashwood
Widow; romantic and impulsive like Marianne; loving mother who struggles with reduced circumstances; tends toward sensibility over sense.
Elinor (eldest, 19)
Sensible, rational, emotionally restrained; becomes her mother's counselor; suffers privately over Edward; embodies "sense."
Marianne (17)
Passionate, romantic, emotionally expressive; values feeling and authenticity over social propriety; embodies "sensibility."
Margaret (13)
Youngest sister; minor character who shares Marianne's romantic temperament; largely remains in the background.
John Dashwood
Half-brother to the sisters; inherits Norland; weak-willed and greedy; persuaded by his wife to do nothing for his sisters.
Fanny Dashwood
John's wife; cold, selfish, snobbish; rationalizes not helping the Dashwood women; opposes Edward's attachment to Elinor.

The Ferrars Family

Edward Ferrars
Fanny's brother; kind, modest, reserved; secretly engaged to Lucy Steele; loves Elinor but bound by honor to Lucy.
Robert Ferrars
Edward's younger brother; vain, foolish, obsessed with fashion; inherits when Edward is disinherited; steals Lucy from Edward.
Mrs. Ferrars
Imperious mother; controls family fortune; disinherits Edward when he refuses to break engagement to Lucy; cold and snobbish.

Other Key Characters

John Willoughby
Handsome, charming fortune-hunter; rescues Marianne; appears perfect but abandons her for an heiress; seduced Colonel Brandon's ward.
Colonel Brandon
36-year-old gentleman; quiet, honorable, constant; loves Marianne despite her disdain; backstory of tragic first love parallels present.
Lucy Steele
Calculating, manipulative young woman; secretly engaged to Edward; reveals engagement to torture Elinor; ultimately elopes with Robert.
Mrs. Jennings
Kindly, vulgar widow; enjoys matchmaking; genuinely kind to the Dashwoods despite lacking refinement; comic relief who proves generous.
Sir John Middleton
Mrs. Dashwood's cousin; offers them Barton Cottage; hearty, hospitable, loves company and sport; good-natured if not refined.
Eliza Williams
Colonel Brandon's ward; illegitimate daughter of his first love; seduced and abandoned by Willoughby; bears his child.

Key Locations

Norland Park
The Dashwood family estate in Sussex where the novel opens. Upon Henry Dashwood's death, it passes to his son John, displacing Mrs. Dashwood and her daughters.
Barton Cottage
The modest cottage in Devonshire offered to the Dashwood women by Sir John Middleton. Their much-reduced home symbolizes their changed circumstances.
Barton Park
Sir John and Lady Middleton's estate near Barton Cottage. Site of dinners, parties, and social gatherings where the sisters meet Willoughby and Brandon.
Allenham
The estate Willoughby expects to inherit from his elderly relative Mrs. Smith. Willoughby shows Marianne the house improperly before any engagement.
London
Where much of the novel's crisis occurs. Marianne encounters Willoughby's cruel rejection, and Lucy reveals her secret engagement to Edward.
Cleveland
The Palmers' estate in Somerset where Marianne falls dangerously ill. Site of her near-fatal fever and Willoughby's midnight confession to Elinor.
Delaford
Colonel Brandon's estate in Dorsetshire. He gives Edward the parish living there, enabling Edward to marry after his disinheritance. Marianne's future home.

December

Elinor and Marianne go to London with Mrs. Jennings; Marianne writes to Willoughby repeatedly
Mid-January
Willoughby cruelly snubs Marianne at party; sends harsh letter denying attachment; announces engagement to Miss Grey
Late January

February
Edward's engagement to Lucy revealed publicly; Mrs. Ferrars disinherits Edward in favor of Robert
March
Colonel Brandon offers Edward the living at Delaford; journey home via Cleveland
April
Marianne walks in rain; develops dangerous fever at Cleveland; Willoughby arrives to confess to Elinor
May
Lucy elopes with Robert Ferrars; Edward freed from engagement; proposes to Elinor
Summer
Edward and Elinor marry; Marianne gradually comes to appreciate and love Colonel Brandon
Following Year
Marianne marries Colonel Brandon; both couples settle happily near each other
