Novel Overview

Sense and Sensibility was Jane Austen's first published novel, though not the first she wrote. Originally drafted as an epistolary novel titled "Elinor and Marianne" in 1795, it was revised and published anonymously in 1811. The novel explores the contrasting temperaments of two sisters‚ÄîElinor's restrained "sense" and Marianne's passionate "sensibility"‚Äîas they navigate love, loss, and the harsh economic realities facing women in Regency England.

"The more I know of the world, the more I am convinced that I shall never see a man whom I can really love. I require so much!"

‚Äî Marianne Dashwood

When Mr. Henry Dashwood dies, his estate passes to his son from a first marriage, leaving his second wife and three daughters‚ÄîElinor, Marianne, and Margaret‚Äîin precarious financial circumstances. Reduced from ¬£4,000 to ¬£500 per year, the Dashwood women must relocate to a small cottage in Devonshire, where the sisters' romantic trials unfold against a backdrop of economic anxiety and social constraint.

Major Themes

Sense vs. Sensibility

The novel's central theme contrasts Elinor's rational self-control with Marianne's emotional openness. Austen questions which approach better navigates life's challenges and whether balance is possible.

Women's Economic Dependence

The Dashwood women's drastic reduction in circumstances illustrates women's vulnerability under inheritance laws. Marriage becomes not just romantic but an economic necessity.

Constancy in Love

Colonel Brandon's patient, enduring love for Marianne contrasts with Willoughby's inconstancy. Elinor's loyalty to Edward despite obstacles exemplifies steadfast devotion.

Deception & Secrecy

Secret engagements (Edward and Lucy, Willoughby and Eliza) drive much of the plot. The novel explores how concealment causes pain and misunderstanding.

Societal Expectations

Characters must navigate rigid social codes governing behavior, courtship, and propriety. Marianne's emotional expressiveness challenges these norms.

Illness as Metaphor

Marianne's near-fatal illness after Willoughby's betrayal represents the dangers of unchecked emotion, while her recovery symbolizes emotional maturation.

Chapter-by-Chapter Discussion Guide

Chapters 1-10: Norland & Displacement

Mr. Dashwood's death, the family's displacement by John and Fanny Dashwood, relocation to Barton Cottage.

  • How does the opening chapter establish the economic vulnerability of women?
  • Analyze the conversation between John and Fanny Dashwood. What does it reveal about greed and rationalization?
  • Compare Elinor and Marianne's reactions to leaving Norland. What do their responses reveal about their characters?
  • How does Austen characterize Edward Ferrars in these early chapters? What attracts Elinor to him?
  • What role does Margaret play in the family dynamic?

Chapters 11-20: Willoughby's Arrival & Romance

Marianne's accident, Willoughby's dramatic rescue, their whirlwind romance, and Colonel Brandon's quiet presence.

  • How does Willoughby's dramatic entrance shape Marianne's perception of him?
  • What warning signs of Willoughby's character does Austen provide to readers but not to Marianne?
  • Why does Marianne dismiss Colonel Brandon? What does this reveal about her values?
  • Compare Elinor's concerns about propriety with Marianne's dismissal of social conventions.
  • What is the significance of Willoughby's abrupt departure for London?

Chapters 21-30: London & Revelations

The sisters visit London, Willoughby's cruel rejection, Lucy Steele's secret engagement to Edward revealed.

  • How does Austen portray Marianne's suffering? Is it excessive or understandable?
  • Analyze Willoughby's letter to Marianne. How does it reveal his character?
  • Why does Elinor keep Lucy's secret about Edward? What does this cost her?
  • Compare how Elinor and Marianne handle heartbreak. What are the strengths and costs of each approach?
  • What does Colonel Brandon's revelation about Eliza Williams add to our understanding of Willoughby?

Chapters 31-40: Cleveland & Illness

Marianne's illness at Cleveland, Willoughby's late-night confession, Colonel Brandon's steadfast support.

  • What does Marianne's illness represent symbolically?
  • Should we sympathize with Willoughby during his confession to Elinor? Why or why not?
  • How has Colonel Brandon demonstrated his love for Marianne throughout the novel?
  • What does Elinor's response to Willoughby's confession reveal about her character?
  • How does Marianne's near-death experience change her perspective?

Chapters 41-50: Resolution

Edward's release from engagement to Lucy, his proposal to Elinor, Marianne's growing appreciation for Colonel Brandon.

  • How does Lucy's elopement with Robert solve Edward's dilemma? Is this satisfying dramatically?
  • Trace Marianne's evolution from despising Colonel Brandon to accepting his suit.
  • Does Marianne's marriage to Colonel Brandon represent compromise or growth?
  • How have both sisters changed by the novel's end? Who has grown more?
  • Is the novel's ending truly happy, or are there lingering complications?

Character Analysis Questions

Elinor Dashwood

  1. Is Elinor's emotional restraint a strength or does it cause her unnecessary suffering?
  2. How does Elinor's role as her mother's confidante affect her own emotional needs?
  3. Compare Elinor's private pain over Edward with Marianne's public suffering over Willoughby.
  4. What does Elinor's response to Willoughby's confession reveal about her capacity for understanding?
  5. Does Elinor represent Austen's ideal, or does the novel critique her excessive self-control?

Marianne Dashwood

  1. Is Marianne's sensibility portrayed as entirely negative, or does Austen value her passion?
  2. How do Marianne's ideas about romantic love set her up for disappointment?
  3. Trace Marianne's emotional journey from Willoughby's abandonment through her illness to her recovery.
  4. Does Marianne's marriage to Colonel Brandon represent a betrayal of her earlier ideals or mature acceptance?
  5. What does Marianne learn about the relationship between feeling and propriety?

Edward Ferrars

  1. Is Edward's secret engagement to Lucy a youthful mistake or a character flaw?
  2. Does Edward's decision to honor his engagement despite loving Elinor make him honorable or passive?
  3. Compare Edward's constancy with Willoughby's inconstancy. What drives each man's choices?
  4. How does Edward's willingness to be disinherited demonstrate his values?
  5. Is Edward worthy of Elinor? Defend your position.

John Willoughby

  1. What makes Willoughby such an effective deceiver of Marianne?
  2. Does Willoughby's confession to Elinor earn him any sympathy? Should it?
  3. How does Willoughby's seduction of Eliza Williams affect our judgment of his treatment of Marianne?
  4. Is Willoughby genuinely in love with Marianne, or merely attracted to her?
  5. What does Willoughby represent in the novel's moral framework?

Colonel Brandon

  1. How does Colonel Brandon's backstory with his first Eliza inform his character?
  2. Is Brandon's love for Marianne creepy given their age difference, or is it genuine and patient?
  3. What qualities make Brandon a suitable match for Marianne despite her initial dismissal?
  4. How does Brandon demonstrate true constancy throughout the novel?
  5. Does Brandon represent Austen's ideal male character? Why or why not?

Thematic Discussion Questions

Sense vs. Sensibility

  1. Does the novel ultimately privilege sense over sensibility, or argue for balance?
  2. How do both Elinor and Marianne learn from each other's approaches by the novel's end?
  3. What are the costs and benefits of each sister's temperament?
  4. How do secondary characters (Lucy, Mrs. Jennings, John Dashwood) embody different combinations of sense and sensibility?

Economic Realities

  1. How does the Dashwood family's financial situation shape every decision in the novel?
  2. Compare the economic motivations behind different characters' marriages (Lucy, Charlotte Palmer, Marianne).
  3. What critique does Austen offer of inheritance laws that favor male heirs?
  4. How does money affect romantic choices and moral decisions throughout the novel?

Constancy & Betrayal

  1. What different forms of constancy appear in the novel (Elinor, Edward, Brandon)?
  2. How do secret engagements function as a form of deception that harms others?
  3. Is Willoughby's betrayal primarily romantic or economic?
  4. What does the novel suggest about the relationship between transparency and true love?

Writing Prompts

  1. Analytical Essay: Argue whether Austen ultimately values sense or sensibility more highly. Use specific textual evidence from both sisters' journeys.
  2. Compare/Contrast: Compare the marriages of Elinor-Edward and Marianne-Brandon. What does each pairing suggest about compatibility and growth?
  3. Close Reading: Analyze Willoughby's confession scene (Chapter 44). How does Austen create sympathy for a character who has behaved badly?
  4. Creative: Write Marianne's letter to Elinor one year into her marriage to Colonel Brandon, reflecting on how her views have evolved.
  5. Character Defense: Defend Edward Ferrars against the charge that he is too passive and indecisive to be an admirable hero.
  6. Thematic Essay: Explore how illness functions symbolically in the novel, particularly Marianne's fever at Cleveland.

Activities & Projects

Individual Activities

  • Character Map: Create a visual diagram showing the two Elizas, Colonel Brandon, and Willoughby's interconnected backstories
  • Financial Analysis: Calculate what the Dashwood women's ¬£500/year income could purchase; compare to other characters' incomes
  • Timeline: Track the timeline of events, noting how Austen manipulates pace during emotional scenes
  • Vocabulary Journal: Create definitions for "sensibility," "constancy," "living," and other period-specific terms

Group Activities

  • Debate: Divide into teams arguing whether Elinor or Marianne had the better approach to love and life
  • Adaptation Comparison: Compare the 1995 film with the 2008 BBC miniseries, analyzing how each portrays the central themes
  • Mock Trial: Put Willoughby on trial for breach of promise and seduction; assign roles for prosecution, defense, witnesses
  • Character Panel: Stage an interview with Elinor, Marianne, and Colonel Brandon five years after the novel ends
